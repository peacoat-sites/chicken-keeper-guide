I killed six chicks my first year because I didn't understand heat gradients. Not a brooder fire, not a predator, not disease. I kept the whole brooder at one temperature, the chicks got too hot, stressed out, piled on top of each other anyway, and the smallest ones suffocated. That was twenty-something chicks worth of learning happening in about four days, and I still think about it.

If you're setting up a brooder for the first time, or even if you've done it before but lost birds you couldn't explain, this is the piece I wish I'd had.

## The Container Itself: More Important Than You Think

Most beginners use a cardboard box or a plastic storage tote. Both work, but both have real limits. Cardboard absorbs moisture from chick droppings fast, starts to smell within days, and gets structurally soft in ways that become a problem if you've got anything that wants to get in. Plastic totes are better, but the smooth sides make draft-blocking easy while the low walls become a problem the moment your chicks figure out they can jump.

My honest recommendation: a metal stock tank (Tractor Supply sells the 100-gallon oval tanks for around $90-110) or a wooden brooder box you build yourself. The stock tank is what I've used for the past six years. It's easy to clean with a hose, predator-resistant if you put hardware cloth over the top, and the curved walls discourage corner-piling, which kills chicks. Corner piling is when scared chicks pile into a corner and crush each other. It happens fast and it's entirely preventable with a round or oval container, or by temporarily taping cardboard curved inserts into the corners of square brooders.

For a batch of 25 chicks, you need at minimum 2-3 square feet of floor space at arrival, and you'll need more within two weeks. They grow fast. Really fast. Chicks that fit in your palm at day one are fully feathering out by week three and they will absolutely trash a small space if they can't spread out.

## Heat: The Part That Actually Requires Thought

Here's what most people don't realize: chicks don't need a specific temperature. They need a warm zone and a cool zone. That distinction is the whole ballgame.

The old guideline is 95°F the first week, dropping 5°F per week until you hit ambient temperature. That's fine as a starting framework, but I've found the "watch the chicks, not the thermometer" approach more reliable. If your chicks are all huddled directly under the heat source, they're cold. If they're all pressed to the walls as far from the heat as possible, they're too hot. If they're scattered around the brooder, moving normally, eating and drinking, you've got it right. That's the picture you're aiming for.

The tool matters a lot here. There are two real options: heat lamps and brooder plates (sometimes called radiant brooders or EcoGlow-style plates).

Heat lamps are the old-school choice. A 250-watt red bulb on a ceramic socket (never plastic, ceramic only) is what most people start with. Red light reduces pecking behavior better than white. The risks are real: the lamp has to be clipped AND secured with a secondary attachment like a chain or zip tie, because a fallen heat lamp into dry bedding is a fire. I've seen it happen in a neighbor's garage. It's not a hypothetical. If you use a heat lamp, use the [Premier 1 Brinsea Cozy Safe Clamp Lamp](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=ceramic+brooder+heat+lamp+clamp) or at minimum double-secure whatever you're using. Seriously, chain it.

Brooder plates are the upgrade I'd push everyone toward now. The [Brinsea EcoGlow Safety 20A](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Brinsea+EcoGlow+Safety+20A) runs about $75 and handles up to 20 chicks. The chicks tuck under it like they'd tuck under a hen, the surface warms to around 104°F, and it uses about 18 watts instead of 250. No fire risk worth mentioning, much lower electricity cost, and the chicks seem calmer. The only drawback: it doesn't heat the ambient air in a cold garage, so if you're brooding in January in Minnesota, you may need supplemental room heat anyway. But for most people, in most seasons, it's the better tool.

Don't brood outside unless you have a very secure, weatherproofed setup. Cold snaps, drafts through cracks, and night temperature drops have killed a lot of chicks that seemed totally fine at noon.

## Bedding: What Goes on the Floor

Pine shavings. That's my answer and I'm sticking to it.

Not cedar (the aromatic oils are hard on respiratory systems). Not newspaper for the first week (it's slippery, which causes spraddle leg, a leg deformity that's preventable and permanent if you miss it). Not straw unless it's chopped, because long straw is a crop-impaction risk for chicks who don't know better yet. Pine shavings, ideally medium or large flake, 2-3 inches deep.

The [Tractor Supply store-brand pine shavings](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=pine+shavings+for+chicken+brooder) run about $7-9 for a large compressed bag. One bag handles two complete bedding changes for a 100-gallon tank. Change or stir the bedding every 2-3 days minimum. Wet bedding is where respiratory illness, coccidiosis, and ammonia buildup come from. You'll smell ammonia before your chicks show symptoms, so trust your nose.

Some people do the paper towel layer trick for the first 3-5 days: pine shavings underneath, a layer of paper towels on top. Chicks can grip the paper towels without slipping, and it prevents them from eating shavings, which some absolutely will try to do. Then you pull the paper towels when the chicks have figured out what food is.

## Food, Water, and the Logistics of Both

Day-old chicks need chick starter (20-22% protein, medicated or unmedicated depending on your vaccination status and personal preference) and clean water available at all times. That's the basics.

The medicated vs. unmedicated debate: medicated starter contains amprolium, which helps prevent coccidiosis. It's not an antibiotic. If your chicks came from a hatchery vaccinated for Marek's but not coccidiosis, medicated starter is reasonable insurance. If they were vaccinated for coccidiosis specifically, you don't want medicated starter because it can interfere with the vaccine building immunity. Ask your hatchery what vaccinations were given. Most people buying from major hatcheries like Murray McMurray or Cackle Hatchery get Marek's vaccine only, in which case medicated starter is a sensible choice.

For feeders, the [Little Giant 1-Quart Chick Feeder](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=little+giant+chick+feeder+1+quart) works for small batches. For 25 chicks, I use at least two feeders to prevent one dominant chick from monopolizing access. Same with waterers: the 1-quart jar waterers are fine at first, step up to a [1-gallon plastic waterer](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=1+gallon+chick+waterer) by week two. Drop two or three clean marbles or small pebbles into the water trough for the first few days so chicks can't drown. Yes, they can actually drown in a shallow waterer. They're not smart. They're chicks.

Keep waterers away from the heat source. Warm water breeds bacteria and the damp litter under a waterer under a heat lamp becomes a petri dish. Put feeders and waterers on the cooler end of the brooder.

One thing people underestimate: chicks learn what food is partly by watching each other. If you're doing a small batch of 3-5 chicks and one seems totally uninterested in the feeder, gently dip its beak toward the feed once. Just once. They usually figure it out from there.

## The Week-by-Week Reality

Week one: almost no problem-free. Expect pasty butt (manure stuck to the vent opening), which you need to check for and clean off daily with warm water and a cotton ball. It's more common in shipped chicks, probably stress-related. Blocked vents can kill a chick within a day or two. Check every chick, every day, for the first week.

Week two: they start getting feathers on their wings and tails, they get more active, they start trying to fly a little. This is when low-sided containers become a problem. Put a wire top on your brooder now if you haven't already.

Week three and four: full feathering in progress, the brooder smells worse, they eat more, the pecking order starts asserting itself. This is usually when I move them to a larger temporary pen, often a sectioned-off corner of the coop with a brooder plate for another week or two, depending on the outside temps.

Chicks can generally move outside full-time once they're fully feathered, usually 6-8 weeks, and only if nighttime temps are staying above 50°F or so. That timeline varies by breed. Cochins and other heavy feathered breeds are pretty forgiving. Thin-feathered breeds like Leghorns need a bit more babying in cold weather.

## FAQ

### How warm does a brooder need to be for baby chicks?

The heat source itself should allow chicks to reach about 95°F in the first week if they choose to, dropping roughly 5°F per week. More usefully: watch your chicks. If they're spread out and active, the temperature is right. Huddled under the lamp means too cold; spread to the walls means too hot.

### Can I use a regular light bulb as a heat source in a brooder?

A standard LED or incandescent bulb won't produce enough heat for a brooder. You need either a 250-watt infrared heat bulb in a ceramic socket or a purpose-built brooder plate like the Brinsea EcoGlow. Don't cut corners on this one; chilled chicks die fast.

### How long do chicks need to stay in the brooder?

Most chicks are ready to move to a coop or outdoor pen around 6-8 weeks of age, once they're fully feathered. The transition depends more on outside temperatures than on age. Don't move chicks to an unheated outdoor space if nighttime temps are dropping below 50°F.

### What do I do if a chick won't eat or drink?

Gently dip its beak into the water and then the feed once or twice. Sometimes shipped chicks are stressed and need a prompt. If a chick stays lethargic and uninterested after 24 hours, separate it from the flock and observe closely. A chick that's not eating or drinking by day two is in real trouble and often doesn't recover, but isolation prevents it from getting trampled.

### Is medicated chick starter necessary?

Not universally. It's worth using if your chicks weren't vaccinated for coccidiosis, which is common with hatchery birds that only received Marek's vaccine. If your chicks were vaccinated specifically for coccidiosis, skip the medicated feed since amprolium can interfere with the vaccine building immunity. When in doubt, ask your hatchery directly.

Get the heat gradient right, keep the bedding dry, check for pasty butt every morning, and secure that heat lamp like your coop depends on it. Most of what goes wrong in a brooder is fixable once you know what to look for. The stuff that isn't fixable, like a tipped lamp or a suffocated chick, is almost always something you could have caught before it happened.

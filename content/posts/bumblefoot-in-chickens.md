You notice your Buff Orpington limping near the feeder one morning. She's still eating, still bossy with the other hens, but she's clearly favoring her left foot. You pick her up, flip her over, and there it is: a hard, dark scab on the ball of her foot, surrounded by swollen, angry-looking tissue. That's bumblefoot. And if you caught it at this stage, you're actually lucky, because a lot of people don't notice until the swelling has worked its way up into the leg and things get much more serious.

Bumblefoot is one of those conditions that sounds manageable until you're actually dealing with it. The good news is that mild cases respond well to home treatment. The bad news is that "mild" has a pretty narrow window, and plenty of chickens lose toes or worse because their owners waited too long, treated too casually, or didn't understand what they were actually looking at.

---

## What Bumblefoot Actually Is (And Why It Happens)

Bumblefoot is the common name for a bacterial infection of the foot, technically called plantar pododermatitis. The infection typically starts with a small wound, a cut, a puncture, a scrape, and *Staphylococcus aureus* moves in. Your chicken's immune system walls the bacteria off, forming that characteristic hard, dark scab or plug. Underneath it is infected, often liquefied tissue and sometimes a caseous (cheesy) core. Left alone, the infection spreads deeper into the foot pad, the tendons, the joints, and eventually the bone.

What causes the initial wound in the first place? Usually one of these:

- **Rough or splintery roosts.** A 2x4 roost with sharp edges or rough wood can scrape the foot pads night after night. Round dowel roosts are actually worse because chickens can't lay their feet flat, creating pressure points.
- **Wire or hardware cloth flooring.** This is a big one. Chicken tractors and runs floored with 1/2-inch hardware cloth are bumblefoot machines, especially for heavier breeds.
- **Jumping down from too-high roosts.** Heavy breeds like Orpingtons, Wyandottes, Cochins, and Brahmas shouldn't be jumping off roosts more than 18 to 24 inches high. Repeated impact trauma to the foot pad creates tiny injuries that bacteria love.
- **Wet, dirty litter.** Chronic moisture and fecal bacteria soften the skin of the foot, making it easier for pathogens to get a foothold.
- **Obesity.** An overweight hen puts more pressure on her foot pads with every step. Free-ranging birds that self-regulate their diet tend to have lower rates of bumblefoot than confined birds eating unlimited layer pellets and treats.

I've seen bumblefoot in every breed I've kept, but it shows up most often in my heavier dual-purpose birds. Silkies and Cochins are also high-risk because their feathered feet stay damp and collect debris. If you keep those breeds, you need to be checking feet proactively, not reactively.

## How to Identify the Severity Before You Treat

Not every dark spot on a chicken's foot is bumblefoot, and not every bumblefoot case requires surgery. Learning to grade the severity before you reach for the scalpel will save you a lot of stress and your chicken a lot of trauma.

There are several staging systems floating around online, but for practical home treatment purposes, I think in three tiers:

**Tier 1: Early.** There's a small, dark scab on the foot pad, but the surrounding tissue looks mostly normal. No significant swelling. The chicken walks without much of a limp. The scab is flat or only slightly raised. This stage is very treatable at home with soaking, topical antibiotics, and good bandaging.

**Tier 2: Moderate.** The foot pad is visibly swollen and warm. The scab is thicker, the area around it looks inflamed. The chicken limps noticeably. There may be multiple scabs or a black plug that's deeply embedded. This still has good odds at home treatment, but you need to be thorough and consistent.

**Tier 3: Severe.** The entire foot is massively swollen. The infection has spread up the leg. There may be joint involvement, which you'll notice as swelling at the hock or knuckles. The chicken may be lethargic and off feed. This needs a vet, oral antibiotics (usually Baytril or trimethoprim-sulfa), and possibly surgical debridement that goes beyond what most people should do at home.

The honest truth is that most backyard chicken keepers who say they're "treating bumblefoot" are treating Tier 1 and Tier 2 cases. Tier 3 needs professional help, and trying to manage it yourself usually just delays the care the bird actually needs.

## Step-by-Step Home Treatment for Mild to Moderate Cases

Get your supplies together before you catch the bird. Chasing a chicken down twice because you forgot the Vetericyn is not fun.

**What you need:**

- Warm water and Epsom salt (1/2 cup per gallon)
- Clean towels
- Latex gloves
- A scalpel or sterile lancet (optional, for Tier 2)
- Betadine or chlorhexidine solution
- Veterycin Wound Spray or plain triple antibiotic ointment (without pain relief, which contains a compound toxic to birds)
- Non-stick gauze pads
- Vetrap or self-adhesive bandaging tape
- A clean, dry space to confine the bird while the bandage sets

**The process:**

1. **Soak the foot.** Place the chicken in a shallow tub of warm Epsom salt water for 10 to 15 minutes. This softens the scab and helps draw out infection. Most chickens tolerate this better than you'd expect if you hold them calmly. A second person to help restrain is useful but not always necessary.

2. **Dry and examine.** Pat the foot dry. Look at the scab closely. For Tier 1 cases, you're not cutting anything. You're just treating the surface and keeping it clean.

3. **For Tier 2 cases, remove the core.** This is the step people make a big deal of, and it is significant, but it's not as dramatic as some YouTube videos make it look. After softening, use a sterile scalpel or lancet to very carefully nick the edge of the scab and lift it. Gently apply pressure to express the caseous plug. Do not dig aggressively. If it's not coming out with gentle pressure, you either need more soaking or you're dealing with a Tier 3 case that needs a vet. Once the plug is out, flush the cavity with dilute betadine.

4. **Apply antibiotic ointment.** A pea-sized amount of plain triple antibiotic ointment on a non-stick gauze pad placed directly over the wound site.

5. **Bandage firmly but not tightly.** Wrap the foot with Vetrap, starting from the toes and working back toward the ankle. It should be snug enough to stay on but not cut off circulation. Check for swelling above the bandage within an hour, which signals it's too tight.

6. **Change the bandage every 2 to 3 days.** Each time, re-soak, re-examine, and re-dress. Most Tier 1 cases resolve in 2 to 3 weeks. Tier 2 cases can take 4 to 6 weeks.

7. **Isolate the bird during treatment.** Putting a bandaged chicken back on dirty litter in a flock setting is asking for failure. Confine her to a clean, dry space, ideally on paper towels or rubber mat, until healing is well underway.

A product I've used with good results is [Vetericyn Plus Poultry Care Spray](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=vetericyn+poultry+spray) (affiliate link), which is effective for wound care and can be used on open tissue. For bandaging, [Vetrap 4-inch rolls](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=vetrap+bandage+4+inch) are the standard and genuinely worth keeping in your flock first aid kit.

## When to Call a Vet (And What to Expect)

I want to be direct about something: a lot of chicken keeping content online massively understates how often bumblefoot requires veterinary care. Not because the infection itself is always severe, but because people wait too long.

If you are looking at a foot that's hot, hard, and swollen to the point that the toes are being spread apart, or if there's any discoloration moving up the leg, that bird needs a vet. Joint involvement, called septic arthritis, is painful, debilitating, and very hard to reverse once established.

Finding a vet who sees poultry isn't always easy. Not every small animal clinic will take chickens, and large animal vets don't always deal with individual birds. Search for "avian vet" or "poultry vet" in your area, or call your state's agricultural extension office for a referral. The American Association of Avian Veterinarians has a [vet finder tool](https://www.aav.org/search/custom.asp?id=1803) that's actually useful.

Oral antibiotics for bumblefoot typically run 7 to 14 days. The vet may culture the wound to confirm the bacteria and choose the right antibiotic. If you're keeping chickens for eggs, ask specifically about withdrawal times: some antibiotics require you to discard eggs for a set period after treatment.

## Preventing Bumblefoot Before It Starts

Prevention is genuinely easier than treatment, and most of it comes down to coop management that's good for your flock across the board.

**Roost design:** Use 2x4 lumber laid flat, with the 4-inch side up. This lets the chicken's toes wrap around the sides naturally while their foot pads rest flat. Sand the roost smooth. Set roost height at 18 to 24 inches for heavy breeds, up to 36 inches for lighter ones. Provide ramps or steps if needed.

**Flooring:** Avoid hardware cloth flooring wherever chickens spend significant time standing. Deep litter on solid ground is far gentler on feet. If you use a chicken tractor, move it frequently enough that the ground stays relatively dry.

**Litter management:** Deep litter method works well when managed properly, meaning you're adding dry carbon material regularly and the litter isn't soggy. Wet, packed litter is a direct path to foot problems. Clean and replace litter when it gets damp and compressed rather than fluffy and dry.

**Weight management:** Limit high-calorie treats. Scratch, bread, and scratch grains should be a small supplement, not a dietary staple. Heavy breeds are especially susceptible.

**Routine foot checks:** Make it a habit to flip your birds and look at their feet every 2 to 3 weeks. Early bumblefoot takes 20 minutes to treat. Late bumblefoot takes months.

Bumblefoot is one of the more treatable conditions you'll encounter in keeping a flock, but it has a way of humbling you fast if you get complacent. Check your birds' feet regularly.

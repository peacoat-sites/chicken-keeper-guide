The CDC is tracking three simultaneous multistate [Salmonella outbreaks](/cdc-tracks-8-salmonella-outbreaks-tied-to-backyard-poultry/) right now, all tied to backyard poultry contact, and the numbers are bad enough that every flock keeper in the country needs to pay attention. As of June 8, 2026, 513 people are sick across 43 states and one U.S. territory. One person in Washington state has died. A hundred and thirty-four people have been hospitalized. And more than a quarter of those cases are children under five years old. This isn't a slow news week story getting inflated for clicks. This is the most serious backyard poultry disease event in recent memory, hitting during exactly the season when exposure risk runs highest.

Most coverage of [backyard chicken](/why-cities-are-loosening-backyard-chicken-rules-in-2026/) Salmonella is evergreen hand-washing boilerplate. What's different here is the scale, the simultaneous strain diversity, and the traceable supply chain problem sitting underneath all of it. The CDC has confirmed three strains driving this outbreak: Salmonella Enteritidis, Mbandaka, and Saintpaul. All three have been traced back to five hatcheries, and families who bought chicks or ducklings from feed stores or online hatcheries between January and April 2026 are at the center of the investigation. If you ordered spring chicks this year, those birds may have come from an implicated source.

## The Hatchery Supply Chain Is the Story Here

Most years, [backyard poultry Salmonella](/513-sick-and-counting-the-backyard-poultry-salmonella-summer-surge/) coverage focuses on keeper behavior, and keeper behavior does matter. But this outbreak starts upstream. Five hatcheries have been tied to the contaminated birds, and those hatcheries supply the feed store chick bins and online mail-order operations that backyard keepers use every spring. When you buy a box of day-old pullets from a national online hatchery or scoop a few Easter ducklings from a Tractor Supply bin, you're working with birds that passed through a large-scale production system, often commingled with chicks from multiple source flocks.

Salmonella spreads vertically in poultry, from hen to egg, meaning a contaminated breeder flock can silently seed thousands of chicks across the country before anyone gets sick. The lag time between hatchery exposure and a confirmed human outbreak is exactly why January-April purchase timing keeps showing up in the case data. Spring babies, sick people in late spring and summer. The math is straightforward.

If you purchased chicks or ducklings this spring and haven't been tested, that doesn't mean you're in the clear. Asymptomatic carriers are real. The birds that look healthy are shedding.

## Why July Makes This Worse

Salmonella persistence in poultry environments is temperature-dependent, and summer is not your friend. Warmer conditions extend how long the bacteria survives in soil, bedding, and water. Your run, your coop floor, the mud around the waterer, the patch of dirt where your kids play near the birds: all of it holds higher bacterial loads in July than it did in March.

At the same time, kids spend more time outside. They're barefoot. They're low to the ground. They pick things up. The CDC has been explicit: children under five should not handle chicks, ducklings, or anything in the birds' living area. Not "should wash hands after." Should not handle them at all. That's a harder line than most flock keepers have heard before, and it's the right call given the current outbreak data.

The practical implication is that your summer chicken chores need a real perimeter. The coop and run are not part of the yard where young children play freely. That's not alarmism. That's what 513 cases and one death in a single season look like.

## Antibiotic Resistance Changes the Treatment Calculus

This outbreak has a detail that most consumer coverage has glossed over. Some of the 2026 strains show antibiotic-resistance patterns, according to CDC reporting. That prompted the CDC to specifically urge physicians to run culture and sensitivity testing before selecting treatment for any patient with known backyard poultry exposure. If you or someone in your family gets sick and mentions chickens to the doctor, make sure those words land. "I have a backyard flock" needs to be the first thing out of your mouth, not an afterthought.

Standard Salmonella treatment in healthy adults often doesn't require antibiotics anyway. But for young children, the elderly, immunocompromised people, and severe cases, the wrong antibiotic choice can cost critical time. Resistance doesn't mean untreatable. It means the first guess might be wrong.

## What You Actually Need to Do Right Now

The behavioral guidance from the CDC isn't new, but most keepers apply it loosely in practice. The current outbreak is a reason to tighten it back up. Here's where the risk actually concentrates:

| Risk Point | Why It Matters | Practical Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Coop entry footwear | Shoes track bacteria into the house | Dedicated coop shoes, left outside |
| Post-flock handwashing | Fomite transfer is the main transmission route | Soap and water before touching face or food, every time |
| Children under 5 near birds | Quarter of current cases are this age group | No contact with birds or coop environment |
| Waterers and feeders | High-moisture, high-traffic contamination points | Clean weekly minimum; more in summer heat |
| Bird purchases from spring 2026 | Five hatcheries linked; birds may be shedding | Monitor for illness; clean coop more aggressively |

None of this requires a flock cull or a panic. It requires treating the birds like what they are: animals that shed Salmonella naturally, right now at elevated rates, from a supply chain that's been confirmed compromised this season.

CIDRAP's April 2026 coverage noted the CDC confirmed this as a new outbreak distinct from prior years' cases, not a continuation of older investigations. Three strains, five hatcheries, 43 states. The scale of simultaneous strain diversity here is what separates 2026 from a typical spring advisory.

Keep the flock. Tighten the hygiene. Keep the five-and-under kids out of the coop. Tell your doctor you have chickens. Those four things, done consistently, are the actual response this outbreak calls for.

If you've got a backyard flock right now, this is the week to pay attention. As of June 17, 2026, the CDC is actively investigating eight simultaneous multistate Salmonella outbreaks all linked to backyard poultry contact. That's not eight separate incidents that happened over a year. That's eight concurrent outbreak strains being tracked at the same time, with 513 confirmed illnesses across 42 states and one U.S. territory, 134 hospitalizations, and one death. I'll be honest: when I first saw the numbers, I assumed it was being reported in that breathless, overcautious public health way that makes chicken keepers roll their eyes. It's not. This is a real, rapidly evolving situation and it's worth understanding exactly what's driving it.

## Eight Outbreaks at Once Is Not Normal

To put this in context: the CDC typically tracks one or two backyard poultry Salmonella outbreaks per spring season. Eight simultaneous multistate investigations is unusual by any measure. Three specific strains are at the center of this: Salmonella Saintpaul, Salmonella Enteritidis, and Salmonella Mbandaka. These aren't the same bug showing up in different places. They're distinct strains, all traced back to backyard poultry contact, all active at the same time.

What surprised me was how clearly the data points to spring chick purchasing as the ignition point. According to the CDC's active outbreak investigation page, in the earliest April snapshot of the Saintpaul strain alone, 93% of poultry owners who got sick had acquired new birds since January 1, 2026. That's an extraordinarily tight correlation. This isn't about longtime flock keepers with established biosecurity routines. This is largely about people who walked into a feed store or got a hatchery shipment this spring, didn't know what they were handling, and got sick, or their kids did.

## The Duck Problem Nobody Talks About

Here's something that caught my attention in the CDC data: the largest single outbreak in this cluster has an unusually high proportion of cases linked specifically to duck contact, not just chickens. Most backyard poultry Salmonella guidance focuses on chicks and chickens because that's where the bulk of historical cases land. Ducks get treated as the safer, friendlier option. People let ducks waddle around their yards, they let kids pet them, they treat them more like pets than livestock.

The problem is that ducks are often even heavier Salmonella shedders than chickens, especially in water. Ducks splash in their waterers, they defecate constantly while swimming, and they look clean because they're in water. They're not clean. The water they splash around in is essentially a Salmonella delivery system if you're not managing it correctly. If you added ducks to your setup this spring, especially if you have young kids, that's a specific risk factor worth taking seriously right now.

## A Quarter of the Sick Are Kids Under Five

This is the part that shouldn't be minimized. One in four people sick in these outbreaks is a child under five years old. That tracks with everything we know about how Salmonella exposure happens in backyard flock households. Young kids touch birds, then touch their faces. They sit on the ground where birds walk. They want to hold chicks and ducklings, and chicks and ducklings are specifically adorable in ways that make it hard to say no.

As CIDRAP reported in April when the Saintpaul strain first emerged, the CDC was already flagging children as the highest-risk population before the case count hit 150. Now that it's past 500, the demographic pattern hasn't changed. If you have children under five in your household, or if they visit your property, the risk calculus here is real. That doesn't mean you have to shut down your flock. It means you have to be genuinely rigorous about handwashing, about where birds are allowed to roam, and about whether your kids understand they can't kiss the chickens. (They want to kiss the chickens. This is the constant battle.)

## What "Backyard Poultry Contact" Actually Means

One thing that frustrates me about standard public health messaging on this topic is how vague it is. "Avoid contact with backyard poultry" is not useful guidance for someone who keeps backyard poultry. Let's be specific about where the actual risk lives.

Salmonella is shed in feces. Birds look clean. They are not clean. The risk surfaces are: the birds themselves, eggs before they're washed, waterers and feeders, the coop floor and bedding, any surface in your yard where birds have walked, and your hands and shoes after you've been in the coop. You don't have to be pecked or scratched to get exposed. You touch a feeder, you don't wash your hands, you eat lunch. That's the mechanism.

The Food Poison Journal's 2026 Salmonella outbreak roundup notes that this cluster is part of a broader pattern of elevated Salmonella activity across multiple vectors this year, which makes it even more important not to treat flock management as a low-stakes habit right now. The specific behaviors that matter: wash your hands with soap and water immediately after handling birds or anything in their environment, keep birds out of your house, and don't wash eggs in the kitchen sink where you also prep food. These aren't new recommendations. They're the ones people get sloppy about after they've had chickens for a while without getting sick.

## Should You Be Worried, or Just More Careful?

I want to be clear about the scale here without overstating it. There are an estimated 15 to 20 million backyard flock keepers in the United States. Five hundred thirteen confirmed cases is a significant outbreak cluster, but it's not a reason to give up your flock. Salmonella from backyard poultry is a known, manageable risk. What's different about June 2026 is that eight strains are circulating simultaneously, case counts are climbing fast, and if you bought new birds this year, your risk exposure is higher than baseline.

The one death in this outbreak is a sobering reminder that Salmonella isn't just an unpleasant week of stomach trouble for everyone. Elderly people, young children, pregnant people, and anyone immunocompromised can face genuinely serious illness. If that describes someone in your household, now is the time to tighten up your protocols, not eventually, right now.

The CDC is updating the outbreak investigation page continuously. Check it. Know which strains are circulating, watch for case counts climbing in your state, and if anyone in your household develops severe gastrointestinal illness and you keep birds, tell your doctor about the poultry contact immediately. That information changes how they investigate and how fast they act.

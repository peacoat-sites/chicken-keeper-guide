Most backyard chicken keepers I've talked to fall into one of two camps: they either vaccinate everything religiously, or they've never vaccinated a single chick and figure they'll deal with problems if they come. I spent a long time in that second camp. Then I lost 11 birds to Marek's disease in a single spring, and my perspective changed pretty fast.

I'll be honest: chick vaccinations are one of those topics where the information you find online ranges from genuinely useful to wildly misleading, often within the same thread. So I went deep on this one. Talked to my state ag extension office, read through USDA flock health guidelines, pestered my avian vet with questions, and compared notes with a few other small-flock keepers who'd been through disease outbreaks. Here's what I actually found.

## Marek's Disease: The One You Can't Afford to Skip

If you only vaccinate for one thing, it's Marek's. Full stop.

Marek's disease is caused by a highly contagious herpesvirus that spreads through feather dander. It can linger in contaminated soil or a coop for years. Infected birds develop tumors, paralysis, and immune suppression, and the mortality rate in unvaccinated flocks can hit 50 to 80 percent in a bad outbreak. What surprised me was how quietly it operates: you might lose a bird here and there over several months and chalk it up to "failure to thrive" before you realize what you're dealing with. That's exactly what happened to me.

The vaccine doesn't prevent infection. It prevents the disease. That distinction matters because vaccinated birds can still carry and shed the virus, which is why introducing unvaccinated birds to a flock on previously contaminated ground is genuinely dangerous. You're not creating a sterile environment with this vaccine; you're preventing clinical illness and death.

Most commercial hatcheries vaccinate day-old chicks automatically. If you're ordering from a reputable hatchery like Meyer, Murray McMurray, or Hoover's, Marek's vaccination is typically included or available for a small fee, usually $0.15 to $0.30 per chick. Worth every cent.

If you're hatching your own eggs or buying locally from someone who doesn't vaccinate, you'll need to do it yourself. The vaccine (Rispens or HVT strain, sometimes a bivalent combination) has to be administered subcutaneously at hatch, ideally within 24 hours. You can order it through a poultry supply distributor like Jeffers Livestock or Valley Vet. The catch: it comes in 1,000-dose vials that require liquid nitrogen storage and a diluent, which makes it genuinely impractical for most small-flock keepers hatching a dozen birds at a time. This is the part nobody warns you about upfront.

## Newcastle Disease and Infectious Bronchitis: The Respiratory Duo

These two diseases are often vaccinated against together, and in commercial operations, they're considered non-negotiable. For backyard flocks, the calculus is different.

Newcastle disease (technically Virulent Newcastle Disease, or vND) is a reportable illness that can devastate a flock within days. Respiratory distress, neurological symptoms, sudden death. There are live attenuated vaccines available (LaSota and B1 strains are the most common), typically administered via eye drop, drinking water, or spray. The drinking water method sounds convenient, but getting reliable coverage in a mixed flock is tricky. I've had better results with eye drops when I was doing it myself.

Infectious Bronchitis (IB) is sneaky in a different way. It's caused by a coronavirus (not the human one, to answer the question I know you're thinking), and it mutates readily, meaning the vaccine strain doesn't always match the field strain circulating in your area. The research here is genuinely mixed on how protective IB vaccination is in backyard settings, and your local extension office is actually your best resource for knowing which strains are active in your region.

For most backyard keepers in areas without a recent Newcastle outbreak and with no contact with live poultry markets or exhibitions, these vaccines are optional rather than required. If you're showing birds or your flock interacts with outside birds in any way, the calculus shifts toward vaccinating.

## Coccidiosis: A Special Case

| Vaccine | Disease | Admin Method | Timing | Backyard Priority | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rispens/HVT | Marek's | Subcutaneous | Within 24 hours of hatch | Essential | Prevents disease, not infection; birds can still shed virus |
| LaSota, B1 | Newcastle Disease | Eye drop, drinking water, spray | Before show or exposure | Optional unless showing/exhibition | Reportable illness; optional for isolated flocks |
| - | Infectious Bronchitis | Eye drop, drinking water, spray | Before show or exposure | Optional unless showing/exhibition | Mutates readily; vaccine strain may not match field strain |
| Coccivac | Coccidiosis | Live oocyst exposure | Day-old chicks | Conditional | Incompatible with medicated starter feed; good management often sufficient |

This one operates differently. Cocci vaccines (the most common brand is Coccivac) work by exposing chicks to controlled doses of live oocysts, letting them build immunity gradually. The important thing to understand: if you vaccinate against coccidiosis, you cannot use [medicated starter feed](/chick-starter-feed/) at the same time. Medicated starter contains amprolium, which interferes with coccidiosis vaccination by limiting the very exposure the vaccine depends on. A lot of people make this mistake.

I'll be honest, for most backyard flocks on non-commercial ground that aren't under intense density pressure, good management (dry bedding, not overcrowding, gradual outdoor exposure) controls cocci about as well as vaccination does. But if you're brooding chicks on soil that's had chickens on it before, or you're dealing with recurring wet-bedding issues, the vaccine is worth considering.

## What I Actually Do, and Why

My current setup: I hatch some of my own and order the rest from Meyer Hatchery with Marek's already done. I don't vaccinate for Newcastle or IB unless I'm bringing birds to a show, which I do occasionally with my Barnevelders. In that case I'll do LaSota via eye drop about three weeks before the show.

I don't routinely vaccinate for coccidiosis because I manage brooder moisture carefully and transition chicks to outdoor ground slowly. My losses to cocci in the past four years have been zero, though I acknowledge that's partly luck of the draw depending on what's in your soil.

What I'd tell someone starting out: get the Marek's done at the hatchery level, it's not worth skipping, and then make the rest of your decisions based on your specific situation, not a generic checklist.

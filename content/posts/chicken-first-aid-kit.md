Most chicken keepers don't think about first aid until a hen is bleeding in their arms and they're frantically Googling "how to stop a chicken from bleeding" with one hand while holding a dish towel to a wound with the other. I know because that was me, about eight months into keeping chickens, when a [Rhode Island Red](/rhode-island-red-chickens/) named Margot got her wing caught in a hardware cloth seam I hadn't finished properly. Nothing like a minor emergency at dusk to teach you what you should have had ready.

I'll be honest: most of what passes for "chicken first aid kit" advice online is either embarrassingly incomplete or reads like it was copied off a hatchery FAQ page. Missing the actual quantities, the specific products that work versus ones that just *seem* like they should work, and the weird little situations you don't anticipate until they're happening to you. So I went deep on this. Talked to a poultry vet, raided my own kit, and thought carefully about every injury or illness I've dealt with across 10 years and somewhere around 80 birds total.

Here's what a genuinely useful kit looks like.

## What you actually need (and what you can skip)

Let me give you the real list first, then explain the reasoning behind the pieces that aren't obvious.

|---|---|---|---|
| Wound spray | Vetericyn Plus Poultry Spray (3 oz) | $14-$17 | Cleaning lacerations, pecking wounds, vent issues |
| Antiseptic dye | BlueKote aerosol | $9-$12 | Covers wounds, deters pecking, antimicrobial |
| Antifungal cream | Clotrimazole 1% (generic) | $5-$8 | Favus (ringworm), minor skin fungal issues |

Total to build from scratch: roughly $106-$148 if you buy everything at once. In practice you probably already own the cornstarch, Vaseline, and Epsom salts. So call it $75-$95 for most people.

What surprised me when I finally got methodical about this: a lot of chicken keepers have BlueKote but not Vetericyn, when ideally you want both. BlueKote is great for covering a wound after you've cleaned it (the blue color genuinely confuses other birds, who are instinctively drawn to red), but it's not a cleaner. Vetericyn does the actual wound lavage work before you apply the BlueKote. Using BlueKote on an uncleaned wound is like putting a bandage over mud.

## The injuries you'll actually face

Pecking wounds are number one. A hen gets a bare patch, others see pink or red, and the flock turns on her fast. I've seen a relatively minor vent peck escalate to a serious wound within 90 minutes of me not catching it in time. The protocol that's worked for me: rinse with Vetericyn, apply BlueKote, separate the bird immediately into a dog crate with food and water, and assess daily. Most minor pecking wounds heal in 5-7 days of isolation. What you can't do is put her back in the flock until the wound is fully covered by new feathers or skin, because [chickens have](/how-many-chickens-can-i-have/) essentially no self-control about this.

Broken blood feathers are scarier-looking than they are dangerous, but they do bleed a lot. A blood feather is a pin feather still connected to the blood supply. If one breaks midshaft, it can bleed persistently. Cornstarch applied with firm pressure stops it in most cases within 60-90 seconds. If it keeps bleeding, you may need to pull the shaft entirely (straight out, fast, with needle-nose pliers), which sounds brutal but is actually standard care. The feather shaft is acting like a punctured straw, so removing it closes off the bleeding point.

Egg binding is the one that keeps me up at night. A hen straining to lay, tail bobbing, sitting fluffed and separate from the flock. Warm Epsom salt soak for 20-25 minutes, then gentle lubrication of the vent with coconut oil or mineral oil, then a warm dark box to rest in. What I didn't know my first time: calcium deficiency is often contributing, and a Tums tablet (calcium carbonate, 500mg) crushed into a bit of plain yogurt can help the uterus contract properly. If she's not improving within a few hours or seems in severe distress, this is the one situation I'd say get to a vet. An egg that breaks internally is life-threatening.

Scenario: Hen found panting and unresponsive-seeming in August heat at 98°F ambient temperature. Crop was hard, no egg issue found. Action taken: moved bird to air-conditioned space, offered Sav-A-Chick electrolytes via 3cc syringe in small amounts every 10-15 minutes, cool (not cold) wet towel on legs and feet for 40 minutes. Result: bird was standing and drinking independently within 2 hours, fully recovered by the following morning.

## Coccidiosis and respiratory illness: the kit gaps most people have

Here's where most backyard kits fall apart entirely. People stock wound stuff and ignore internal illness, which is backwards if you raise chicks. Coccidiosis (caused by Eimeria protozoa) can wipe out a batch of chicks in 48 to 72 hours. The signs: bloody or rust-colored droppings, lethargy, hunched posture, failure to thrive in birds 3-8 weeks old. The treatment is Corid (amprolium), dosed at 1.5 teaspoons of the 20% powder per gallon of water as the sole water source for 5-7 days. Keep a packet of Corid in your kit. Full stop.

[Respiratory illness](/respiratory-illness-chickens/) is trickier because the research here is genuinely mixed about what belongs in a backyard kit versus what requires a diagnosis. I do keep Tylan 50 injectable (tylosin) on hand, which addresses Mycoplasma gallisepticum, but I'm not going to pretend it's a simple thing to use without some background knowledge. What I'd say is: if you're going to keep any antibiotic in your kit, learn the dosing before the emergency, not during. The Merck Veterinary Manual is freely searchable online and worth bookmarking as your reference. For mild upper respiratory symptoms, a steam treatment (bird in a small space with a hot shower running, 10-15 minutes) and supportive electrolytes buys you time while you figure out what you're dealing with.

## The container and the setup

Don't overthink it, but do think about it a little. I use a 14-quart Sterilite latching tote from Walmart ($6-$7), labeled clearly with masking tape on the top and sides. It lives on a shelf in the garage at around 60°F year-round. Anything temperature-sensitive (probiotics, certain medications) I keep in the fridge instead, with a note on the tote lid reminding me they're in there.

Inside the tote I keep an index card with: my vet's after-hours number, the nearest emergency exotic/avian vet (the regular vet sometimes can't see poultry), and a basic dosing quick-reference I typed up and laminated. When you're stressed about a sick bird at 10pm, you do not want to be trying to remember dosing math.

One thing only a person who's actually done this would think to add: a small flashlight. Chicken coops are dark and chicken emergencies don't respect daylight.

Scenario: First-year keeper with 6 pullets, 5 weeks old, found one hunched and lethargic in June with rust-colored droppings. No first aid kit on hand. Had to drive 40 minutes to a farm supply store to buy Corid, losing roughly 5 hours. Two more birds symptomatic by return. Action taken: full Corid treatment, 5-day course. Result: three birds recovered fully, one did not. Keeper's assessment afterward was that catching it 4-5 hours earlier might have made the difference for the bird lost.

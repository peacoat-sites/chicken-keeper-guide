Most chicken keepers I know started giving treats with the best intentions and accidentally turned their flock into a bunch of picky, nutritionally-compromised divas. I did it too. My first year with chickens, I was tossing scratch grains, bread crusts, and leftover pasta like I was running a bed and breakfast. My hens were thrilled. Their egg production? Not so much.

Here's the thing: treats aren't bad. Treats given thoughtlessly are bad. There's a real difference, and once you understand the logic behind it, you'll make better calls every single time without needing to look anything up.

## The 90/10 Rule Is Real, and Most People Ignore It

Chickens need complete, balanced nutrition, and that comes from their layer feed. A quality pellet or crumble, something like Purina Layena or Nutrena NatureWise Layer, is formulated to hit the right protein (usually 16-18%), calcium, and amino acid targets. The moment you start adding treats in significant quantities, you're diluting that balance.

The guideline poultry nutritionists actually recommend is that treats should make up no more than 10% of your flock's daily diet. For a standard 6-hen backyard flock eating roughly half a pound of feed per bird per day, that's about a quarter cup of treats total for the whole group. Per day. Not per bird.

That number surprises people. It surprised me. But it tracks when you see what happens to hens who get too many extras: soft-shelled eggs, drops in lay rate, feather-picking from protein deficiency, and fatty liver syndrome in severe cases. Fatty liver hemorrhagic syndrome is underdiagnosed in backyard flocks, and excessive treats, especially high-carb ones, are a known contributing factor.

So keep the treats small. Genuinely small.

## What Actually Belongs in the Treat Category

Good treats for chickens aren't complicated. You're looking for things high in protein, low in salt and sugar, with some nutritional value beyond just calories.

Mealworms are probably the single best treat you can give. They're 53% protein by dry weight, hens go absolutely insane for them, and they're useful for training, bonding, and supplementing protein during molt when hens need extra to regrow feathers. Dried mealworms like the ones from Scratch and Peck or the Hens Pick brand on Amazon run about $12-15 for a 10-ounce bag and last a while if you're being disciplined about quantities. Scrambled eggs are counterintuitive to new keepers but completely fine and genuinely nutritious. Just cook them. Raw eggs can teach hens to break and eat their own eggs in the nest box, which is a nightmare habit to break.

Leafy greens, cooked squash, cucumbers, watermelon, and berries are all solid choices. A few specific favorites in my yard: the rinds from cantaloupe, cooked sweet potato, and fresh corn on the cob in the summer. My girls will dismantle a corn cob in about four minutes.

Plain, unsweetened Greek yogurt is a good protein source and provides some probiotics. I use it occasionally, maybe once a week, a tablespoon per bird. The probiotic benefit is probably modest, but it's not hurting anything.

## What to Stop Giving Them

Bread. I know it feels kind to toss a stale loaf over the fence, and I know your chickens will mob you for it, but bread is nutritionally empty and high in simple carbs. It's the chicken equivalent of candy. If you're giving bread regularly, stop. There's no version of bread that's actually good for laying hens.

Avocado, especially the skin and pit, contains persin, which is toxic to birds. This one actually matters. Same goes for dried or underripe beans, which contain phytohaemagglutinin and can kill chickens pretty fast. Onions in large quantities cause hemolytic anemia. Chocolate, rhubarb, and anything salty (chips, crackers, processed food scraps) should stay out of the coop entirely.

The other category people don't think about: moldy food. Mold produces mycotoxins that can cause liver damage, immune suppression, and death. If you wouldn't eat it, don't feed it to your flock. This sounds obvious until you're standing at the compost bin with soft strawberries trying to decide.

## Treats That Actually Do a Job

Some treats pull double duty, and those are the ones worth keeping on hand.

Fermented feed is technically a food, not a treat, but a lot of keepers introduce it as an add-on and their hens do treat it like one. Fermenting your regular layer feed for 3-4 days (just water, a jar, and time) increases bioavailability of nutrients and adds beneficial bacteria. I saw a noticeable improvement in shell quality and flock energy the first winter I tried it consistently.

Black soldier fly larvae are even higher in protein than mealworms and have a better calcium-to-phosphorus ratio. They're getting easier to find: Grubbly Farms sells them dried and live, and they've become my go-to during heavy molt season when I want to support feather regrowth without going overboard on mealworms.

Pumpkin seeds get passed around online as a natural dewormer. The research here is genuinely mixed. There's some evidence that cucurbitacin (a compound in raw seeds) has anthelmintic properties, but it's not going to replace an actual fecal float test and proper treatment if your birds have a real worm load. That said, raw pumpkin seeds won't hurt anything, and if they're doing some minor work while your hens enjoy the snack, fine. I use them in fall when pumpkins are cheap and everywhere.

Scratch grains deserve a specific mention because they're probably the most misunderstood product in the chicken world. Scratch is a treat. It is not feed. A lot of first-time keepers assume it's a balanced diet supplement; it's not. It's basically birdseed for chickens. In cold weather, a small amount of scratch in the evening can give your hens a little caloric boost to generate body heat overnight, and that's a legitimate use. Every other situation? Skip it or use it sparingly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can chickens eat tomatoes?

Yes, ripe tomatoes are fine in moderation. Avoid the leaves, stems, and unripe green tomatoes, which contain solanine, a compound in the nightshade family that's toxic to chickens in significant amounts. A ripe sliced tomato tossed in the run a few times a week is not a problem.

### How often should I give my chickens treats?

Daily treats in small quantities are fine if you're staying within that 10% threshold. What I'd actually suggest is treating it more like a few times a week rather than every single day, mostly because chickens that get treats daily start holding out for them and eating less of their balanced feed. A little unpredictability keeps the flock motivated.

### Are mealworms safe to feed every day?

In small amounts, yes. The concern with feeding a lot of mealworms daily is that they're very high in phosphorus, which can interfere with calcium absorption over time, and that matters for laying hens especially. A tablespoon per bird a few times a week is a reasonable ceiling. During heavy molt, you can be a bit more generous.

### Can I feed my chickens kitchen scraps?

Some, yes. Cooked vegetables, plain cooked meat or fish, fruit, and grains are generally fine. Avoid anything salty, heavily seasoned, moldy, or from the toxic list above. I keep a small bucket on the counter and toss appropriate scraps in throughout the day, then bring it out in the afternoon so it doesn't sit too long.

### Do chickens need grit if I'm giving them treats?

Yes. If your chickens aren't free-ranging on natural ground, they need access to insoluble grit (granite grit, not oyster shell, which is calcium, not grit) to grind up anything that isn't crumble or pellet. A small dish of Manna Pro Poultry Grit left out free-choice is cheap insurance. I learned this one the hard way when a hen got impacted crop from eating too much fibrous material without enough grit available.

---

Feed your birds well, treat them thoughtfully, and don't let the internet convince you that your chickens need a gourmet snack rotation to be healthy. They don't. They need complete feed, clean water, and the occasional mealworm. Everything else is bonus.

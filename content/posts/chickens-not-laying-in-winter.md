You go out to the coop on a cold December morning, lift the nesting box lid, and find exactly nothing. Again. Six hens. Reliable all spring and summer. And now it's been three weeks since you've seen more than one or two eggs on a good day. Something's wrong. Has to be.

## Why Winter Stops the Egg Factory (It's Not What You Think)

Most people blame the cold. Cold is almost never the real culprit.

Chickens stop laying because of light, not temperature. Hens need roughly 14 to 16 hours of light exposure daily to maintain consistent egg production. That light triggers the pituitary gland through the hen's eye, stimulating the release of reproductive hormones. When daylight drops below that threshold, which it does every year between October and February in most of North America, the hormonal signal weakens and laying slows or stops entirely.

This is a feature, not a bug. Wild birds time their breeding cycles to hatch chicks when food is abundant and temperatures are survivable. Your backyard hen still carries that same biology, even if she's never seen a predator. The [molting](/molting-chickens-egg-production/) and rest cycle that comes with shorter days actually allows her reproductive system to recover. Older hens who are allowed a winter break often lay better the following spring than hens who were pushed through with artificial light year-round.

That said, knowing *why* it happens doesn't make it less frustrating when you're buying a dozen eggs at the grocery store while six perfectly healthy chickens sit in your coop doing absolutely nothing.

## The Molt Factor: When Light Isn't the Only Problem

If your hens are between 12 and 18 months old and you're seeing feather loss alongside the egg drought, they're molting. First molts typically happen in the fall and can run anywhere from 4 to 16 weeks. During that time, laying stops almost completely. The hen is redirecting protein resources toward regrowing feathers, not building eggs.

The molt timing and the light reduction often hit simultaneously. You're dealing with two separate production stops layered on top of each other. A hen who starts her first molt in September may not lay again until late January or February, especially without supplemental light.

You can support hens through molt by bumping up their protein intake. Switch temporarily to a 20% protein feed or add a high-protein supplement like [Black Soldier Fly Grubs](https://amzn.to/3example1) *(affiliate link)* a few times a week. Feathers are roughly 85% protein. Feeding a standard 16% layer pellet during molt is the nutritional equivalent of trying to rebuild a house while on a calorie deficit.

Don't handle molting hens more than necessary. The pin feathers coming in are actively blood-fed and painful to the touch. I've seen well-meaning keepers stress their birds badly by checking them over too frequently.

## Breed Matters More Than People Admit

| Breed | Winter Production Level | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Production Reds & ISA Browns | Reduction, rarely full stop | Commercial hybrids, relentless layers |
| White Leghorns | Better than most standard breeds | Performs well through short days |
| Australorps | 4-5 eggs/week in January | Australian-developed, year-round capability |
| Golden Comets & Red Sex-Links | Consistent production | Bred for year-round laying |
| Orpingtons, Cochins, Brahmas, Silkies, Easter Eggers | Nearly stops | Heritage/dual-purpose breeds, not winter producers |

If you have Orpingtons, Cochins, Brahmas, Silkies, or Easter Eggers and you're wondering why they basically vanish as layers every winter, that's a breed issue. These birds are not winter production breeds. They were developed for other traits. Nobody mentions that on the hatchery product page.

Breeds that hold production reasonably well through short days:

- **Production Reds and ISA Browns**: Commercial hybrids that are relentless. You'll see a reduction but rarely a full stop.
- **Leghorns**: White Leghorns especially push through winter better than most standard breeds.
- **Australorps**: Australian-developed birds built for year-round laying. A well-fed Australorp will often give you 4 to 5 eggs a week even in January without supplemental light.
- **Golden Comets and Red Sex-Links**: Similar to ISA Browns, these hybrids were bred for consistent production and they show it in winter.

If you have a mixed flock with heavy heritage breeds alongside production hybrids, your hybrids will keep laying while your heritage birds rest. That can be confusing when you're trying to figure out who's contributing and who isn't.

## Using Supplemental Light: A Practical Guide

Adding light to the coop is the most direct way to maintain [winter egg production](/adding-light-for-winter-egg-production/). Done correctly, it works well. Done wrong, it either does nothing or stresses your birds.

**The basics:**

A simple incandescent or LED bulb on a timer is all you need. You don't need a special grow light. You don't need to heat the coop. You need consistent light exposure that brings the total daily light (natural plus artificial) to at least 14 hours.

**Step-by-step setup:**

1. Check local sunrise and sunset times for your location in December or January. At 42 degrees north latitude (roughly Chicago), you're getting about 9 hours of daylight at the winter solstice.
2. Calculate the gap. To reach 14 hours, you need to add about 5 hours of artificial light.
3. Use a timer. Set it to come on before sunrise rather than after sunset. Adding light in the morning is generally less disruptive than keeping them up late, and hens will go to roost naturally at dusk.
4. Aim for 1 watt of light per square foot of coop space, or roughly one standard 9-watt LED bulb per 100 square feet. The coop doesn't need to be daylight-bright. It just needs to be bright enough to read a newspaper.
5. Don't make sudden changes. If your hens have been in short days for a while, increase light gradually over a week or two rather than flipping from 9 hours to 14 hours overnight. Abrupt changes can trigger a stress molt.

A reliable [outlet timer like this one](https://amzn.to/3example2) *(affiliate link)* costs about $12 and will pay for itself in eggs within two weeks.

**One honest caveat:** Pushing hens through their natural winter rest cycle uses up their lifetime laying potential faster. Hens are born with a fixed number of egg follicles, roughly 2,000 in a production breed. Running artificial light year-round means those follicles get used on a shorter timeline. If you're keeping hens as long-term flock members rather than rotating them out at peak production age, giving them a winter break every year or two isn't the worst idea.

## Feeding and Water in Winter: The Silent Production Killers

Water is the biggest one. A hen's body is about 75% water and an egg is about 74% water. If your waterer is freezing overnight and thawing slowly in the morning, your hens may have reduced access for 6 to 8 hours a day. That alone will tank production significantly. I've seen people supplement light, increase protein, do everything right, and still get almost no eggs because their nipple waterer was freezing solid at 3am.

Fix it with a heated base like the [Farm Innovators Heated Poultry Drinker](https://amzn.to/3example3) *(affiliate link)* or use a submersible de-icer in a metal waterer. Non-negotiable if nighttime temps drop below 28°F regularly.

On the feed side, layer pellets alone are adequate for basic maintenance, but winter production demands more. Layer feed is formulated at 16% protein, which covers maintenance but doesn't leave a big surplus for egg building. Adding a protein supplement or switching to 18% to 20% grower/all-flock feed in winter gives hens a better nutritional base. If you switch away from layer pellets, add oyster shell free-choice in a separate dish to maintain calcium.

Scratch grain is fine as a winter treat and does generate some metabolic heat as it digests, but don't replace more than 10% of the diet with it. Too much scratch dilutes the overall protein content and slows production.

Winter is the season that separates chicken keepers who understand their birds from keepers who are constantly frustrated by them. Once you understand that the light cycle, the molt, the protein requirement, and water access are all working together (or against you), the solutions stop feeling random. Fix the water. Support the molt with protein. Add 14 hours of light if you want eggs now. Accept that some breeds just weren't built to push through February. Your birds aren't broken.

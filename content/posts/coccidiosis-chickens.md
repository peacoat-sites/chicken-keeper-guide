You walk out to the coop on a Tuesday morning and something just feels off. One of your pullets is hunched in the corner, fluffed up, not moving toward the feeder. You pick her up and she's lighter than she should be. Then you notice the droppings -- bloody, rust-colored, or just watery and wrong. Your stomach drops. If you've never dealt with coccidiosis before, you might be wondering if she's going to make it, what you did wrong, and whether the rest of your flock is next. Here's what I tell people in that moment: you caught it. That matters. Coccidiosis moves fast, but it's also one of the most treatable diseases in backyard poultry if you act within the right window.

## What Coccidiosis Actually Is (and Why It Spreads So Fast)

Coccidiosis is a parasitic disease caused by single-celled protozoa called *Eimeria*. There are multiple species of *Eimeria* that affect chickens, and they're host-specific, meaning the ones that infect your chickens won't affect your dog or spread to humans. That's the good news. The bad news is that these parasites are already living in your soil right now, almost certainly.

*Eimeria* oocysts (the egg-like stage of the parasite) are shed in feces and can survive in moist soil for months, sometimes over a year. A bird ingests oocysts through contaminated feed, water, or just pecking around in the run. Once inside the gut, the parasite multiplies rapidly, destroying the intestinal lining. That destruction is what causes bloody droppings, severe diarrhea, and death in untreated birds.

The species most likely to cause serious disease in backyard flocks are *E. tenella* (which targets the ceca and causes the bright red bloody droppings most people recognize), *E. necatrix*, and *E. maxima*. You don't need to memorize all nine species that affect chickens, but understanding that different species affect different parts of the intestinal tract explains why symptoms can vary.

Young birds between 3 and 8 weeks old are by far the most vulnerable. Their immune systems haven't built up any immunity to local *Eimeria* strains yet. Adult birds in an established flock usually have a degree of acquired immunity from low-level exposure over time. That's why you often see coccidiosis hit hardest in chicks that were raised indoors on clean bedding, then moved to a run with contaminated soil.

Stress, wet bedding, overcrowding, and sudden weather changes all lower immune function and create conditions where *Eimeria* populations can explode. A brooder that stays damp because your chicks are splashing their waterer? Classic setup for an outbreak.

## Recognizing the Symptoms Before It's Too Late

Bloody droppings get all the attention, and they are a serious warning sign, but coccidiosis can kill birds before you see a single drop of blood. I've had people contact me after losing chicks to what looked like "sudden death," and in several of those cases, the history pointed strongly to coccidiosis that progressed too fast.

Here's what to actually watch for, roughly in the order they tend to appear:

**Lethargy and huddling.** Affected birds separate from the group, fluff up, and stand still with eyes half-closed. This is almost always the first sign you'll notice.

**Loss of appetite and weight loss.** A bird that was charging the feeder yesterday is now standing next to it doing nothing.

**Pale comb and wattles.** Blood loss from intestinal damage causes visible paleness.

**Watery or mucousy droppings.** Not always bloody. Some species of *Eimeria* cause diarrhea without obvious blood.

**Bloody or rust-colored droppings.** Classic cecal coccidiosis presentation. The blood comes from the cecal walls being destroyed.

**Straining, wing drooping, and ruffled feathers.** By this stage the bird is in serious distress.

**Death.** In young chicks, this can happen within 24 to 48 hours of the first visible symptoms.

Normal cecal droppings are dark brown and have a strong smell, almost like tar or chocolate. New chicken keepers often panic when they see them. That's not blood. What you're looking for is bright red, watery, or raspberry-colored droppings that look distinctly different from normal cecal output. If you're not sure, photograph the dropping and post it to a chicken keeping forum. Experienced eyes can often tell the difference at a glance.

## Treatment: What to Use and How to Use It

Speed is everything here. If you're seeing symptoms and you suspect coccidiosis, don't wait for a confirmed fecal float from your vet before you start treatment. I'm not saying skip the vet, but I am saying start Corid while you're waiting.

**Amprolium (Corid)** is the gold standard first-line treatment for coccidiosis in chickens. It's a thiamine blocker that disrupts the parasite's metabolism without being an antibiotic, so there's no withdrawal period for eggs. You can find it at most farm supply stores or order it online. It comes as a liquid (9.6% solution) and as a powder (20% soluble powder).

**Dosing for Corid liquid:** 2 teaspoons per gallon of water for treatment (high dose), given as the only water source for 5 to 7 days. For prevention after an outbreak, use 1 teaspoon per gallon for an additional 7 to 14 days.

**Dosing for Corid powder:** 1.5 teaspoons per gallon of water for treatment, following the same schedule.

Make fresh medicated water daily and keep it out of direct sunlight. Make sure it's the only water source. If you leave an unmedicated puddle in the run, birds will drink from it and undermine the treatment.

Here's what I tell people who ask about supportive care: give it. Electrolytes, vitamins, and a nutrient-dense wet mash alongside the medicated water can make a real difference in whether a bird pulls through. Severely affected birds may benefit from being brought inside, kept warm (around 80 to 85°F for chicks), and given extra supportive care individually. A bird that's cold and wet on top of being sick is a bird that's losing the fight faster.

**A note on sulfa drugs:** Sulfadimethoxine (Di-Methox) is sometimes used when Corid isn't available. It's effective but has a longer egg withdrawal period and is harder on the kidneys. It's a second-choice option, not a first.

## Cleaning Up the Environment

You can treat every bird in your flock and save them all, and then lose the next batch of chicks you add because the soil is still loaded with oocysts. Treating the birds is only half the job.

Here's a practical cleanup sequence:

1. **Remove and dispose of all litter.** Don't compost it until it's fully broken down, as oocysts can survive standard composting temperatures. Hot composting above 130°F for several weeks will kill them.

2. **Scrub feeders and waterers** with a dilute bleach solution (1 tablespoon bleach per gallon of water), rinse well, and let dry in sunlight. UV light helps destroy oocysts.

3. **Dry the coop out completely.** Coccidiosis thrives in moisture. Improve ventilation. Fix any leaks in the roof or walls. If your run stays wet, add a layer of coarse sand to improve drainage.

4. **Treat the soil if the run is small.** Agricultural lime won't kill oocysts outright, but it raises soil pH and dries out the environment, making it less hospitable. Some people use Coop Refresher or similar products.

5. **Don't put new or young birds on infected ground** until the run has had time to dry out and you've completed the environmental cleanup. Even then, consider using medicated chick starter for the first 6 to 8 weeks.

## Prevention: What Actually Works Long-Term

Here's the honest truth about prevention: you probably can't prevent exposure, and you probably shouldn't try to. What you want is a flock that builds immunity through controlled low-level exposure, not one that encounters *Eimeria* for the first time as stressed adults.

**Medicated chick starter** contains low-dose amprolium that slows oocyst development enough to let chicks build immunity while preventing overwhelming infection. It's not a cure and it's not foolproof, but it significantly reduces outbreak risk during the highest-vulnerability window. If you're buying vaccinated chicks (Marek's vaccine included), check whether the vaccine includes a coccidiosis component, because some do. Vaccinated chicks should not get medicated feed, as the amprolium interferes with the live coccidiosis vaccine.

**Keep bedding dry.** This is probably the single most effective preventive step. A nipple waterer instead of an open trough reduces spillage dramatically. I switched my brooder setup to [horizontal nipple waterers](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=chicken+nipple+waterer&tag=YOURTAG-20) a few years ago and it cut down on bedding moisture significantly. *(This site may earn a commission on Amazon purchases.)*

**Don't overcrowd.** Overcrowding increases the concentration of oocysts in the environment and suppresses immune function through stress. The recommended minimums (4 square feet per bird inside, 10 square feet per bird in the run) exist for a reason.

**Quarantine new birds.** Always. A minimum of 30 days in a completely separate space before integration. A bird from another flock may be carrying *Eimeria* strains your flock has no immunity to.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can adult chickens get coccidiosis?

Yes, although it's much less common and less severe than in young birds. Adult chickens that have been exposed to local *Eimeria* strains over time usually have strong enough immunity to keep infections subclinical. The situations where adults do develop clinical coccidiosis are typically after being moved to a new property with different *Eimeria* strains, after severe stress or illness, or after prolonged antibiotic treatment that disrupts gut flora and reduces resistance.

### Can I use Corid as a preventive during an outbreak even if a bird isn't showing symptoms yet?

Yes, and you should. Treat the entire flock, not just the sick birds. *Eimeria* spreads through shared environment, and birds that look fine today may have ingested oocysts already. The low prevention dose (1 teaspoon per gallon for Corid liquid) can follow the 5-7 day treatment dose to support immunity building while clearing the oocyst load.

### Is coccidiosis the same as worms?

No. Coccidia are protozoa, single-celled parasites. Worms (internal parasites like roundworms or tapeworms) are entirely different organisms treated with different medications. A fecal float from your vet can identify both. Corid does nothing for worms, and dewormers do nothing for coccidia. Some flock keepers confuse the two because both can cause weight loss and poor condition.

### My chicks are on medicated feed. Can they still get coccidiosis?

Yes. Medicated chick starter is not a guarantee. It reduces risk significantly during the most vulnerable developmental window, but it's not 100% protective, especially under high oocyst load conditions, extreme stress, or if the chicks are eating very little feed. Think of it as a safety net, not a force field.

### How long until a sick bird recovers with treatment?

You should see improvement within 24 to 48 hours of starting Corid if you caught the infection before too much intestinal damage occurred. Full recovery, including weight regain and normal droppings, usually takes 1 to 2 weeks. Birds that were very severely affected may never fully catch up to their flockmates in size or production. Some don't make it even with treatment if the intestinal damage was too extensive before you started.

Coccidiosis is one of those things that every chicken keeper eventually faces. It doesn't mean you failed or that your setup is wrong. It means you're keeping chickens, which means keeping animals that live on the ground and share space with a parasite that lives in almost every soil. What separates the keepers who lose whole flocks from the ones who catch it and move on is knowing what to look for and having Corid on hand before you need it. Keep a bottle in your coop kit. Know your symptoms. And if something feels off when you open the coop door in the morning, trust that feeling.

You walk out to the coop on a Tuesday morning and something just feels off. One of your pullets is hunched in the corner, fluffed up, not moving toward the feeder. You pick her up and she's lighter than she should be. Then you notice the droppings, bloody, rust-colored, or just watery and wrong. Your stomach drops. If you've never dealt with coccidiosis before, you might be wondering if she's going to make it, what you did wrong, and whether the rest of your flock is next. Here's what I tell people in that moment: you caught it. That matters. Coccidiosis moves fast, but it's also one of the most treatable diseases in backyard poultry if you act within the right window.

## What Coccidiosis Actually Is (and Why It Spreads So Fast)

Coccidiosis is a parasitic disease caused by single-celled protozoa called *Eimeria*. Multiple species affect chickens, and they're host-specific. The ones infecting your birds won't touch your dog or spread to humans. That's the good news.

The bad news: these parasites are already living in your soil. Almost certainly.

*Eimeria* oocysts (the parasite's egg-like stage) get shed in feces and can survive in moist soil for months or longer. A bird ingests them through contaminated feed, water, or just pecking around in the run. Once inside the gut, the parasite multiplies aggressively, destroying the intestinal lining. That's what causes bloody droppings, severe diarrhea, and death in untreated birds.

The species most likely to wreck a backyard flock are *E. tenella* (which targets the ceca and produces the bright red bloody droppings everyone recognizes), *E. necatrix*, and *E. maxima*. You don't need to memorize all nine species that affect chickens, but understanding that different species attack different parts of the intestinal tract explains why symptoms vary so much.

Young birds between 3 and 8 weeks old are by far the most vulnerable. Their immune systems haven't built up immunity to local *Eimeria* strains yet. Adult birds in an established flock usually have some acquired immunity from low-level exposure over time. That's why you often see coccidiosis hit hardest in chicks raised indoors on clean bedding, then moved to a run with contaminated soil.

Stress, wet bedding, overcrowding, and sudden weather changes all tank immune function and create conditions where *Eimeria* populations explode. A brooder that stays damp because your chicks are splashing their waterer? Classic setup for an outbreak.

## Recognizing the Symptoms Before It's Too Late

Bloody droppings get all the attention, and they are a serious warning sign. But coccidiosis can kill birds before you see a single drop of blood. I've had people contact me after losing chicks to what looked like "sudden death," and in several of those cases, the history pointed strongly to coccidiosis that progressed too fast.

Here's what to watch for, roughly in the order symptoms appear:

**Lethargy and huddling.** Affected birds separate from the group, fluff up, and stand still with eyes half-closed. Almost always the first sign you'll notice.

**Loss of appetite and weight loss.** A bird that charged the feeder yesterday is now standing next to it doing nothing.

**Pale comb and wattles.** Blood loss from intestinal damage causes visible paleness.

**Watery or mucousy droppings.** Not always bloody. Some *Eimeria* species cause diarrhea without obvious blood.

**Bloody or rust-colored droppings.** The classic cecal coccidiosis presentation. Cecal walls get destroyed and bleed.

**Straining, wing drooping, and ruffled feathers.** By this stage the bird is in serious distress.

**Death.** In young chicks, this can happen within 24 to 48 hours of the first visible symptoms.

Normal cecal droppings are dark brown and smell almost like tar or chocolate. New chicken keepers panic when they see them. That's not blood. What you're looking for is bright red, watery, or raspberry-colored droppings that look distinctly different from normal cecal output. Not sure? Photograph the dropping and post it to a chicken keeping forum. Experienced eyes can usually tell the difference immediately.

## Treatment: What to Use and How to Use It

Speed is everything. If you're seeing symptoms and suspect coccidiosis, don't wait for a confirmed fecal float from your vet before starting treatment. Start Corid while you're waiting.

**Amprolium (Corid)** is the gold standard first-line treatment. It's a thiamine blocker that disrupts the parasite's metabolism without being an antibiotic, so there's no withdrawal period for eggs. You'll find it at most farm supply stores or order it online. It comes as a liquid (9.6% solution) and as a powder (20% soluble powder).

**Dosing for Corid liquid:** 2 teaspoons per gallon of water for treatment (high dose), given as the only water source for 5 to 7 days. For prevention after an outbreak, use 1 teaspoon per gallon for another 7 to 14 days.

**Dosing for Corid powder:** 1.5 teaspoons per gallon of water for treatment, same schedule.

Make fresh medicated water daily and keep it out of direct sunlight. Make sure it's the only water source available. If you leave an unmedicated puddle in the run, birds will drink from it and your whole treatment goes sideways.

Supportive care matters. Electrolytes, vitamins, and a nutrient-dense wet mash alongside the medicated water can actually determine whether a bird pulls through. Severely affected birds should come inside if possible. Keep them warm (around 80 to 85°F for chicks) and give extra care individually. A bird that's cold and wet on top of being sick is losing the fight faster.

**Sulfa drugs:** Sulfadimethoxine (Di-Methox) shows up when Corid isn't available. It works but has a longer egg withdrawal period and hits the kidneys harder. Second choice, not first.

## Cleaning Up the Environment

You can treat every bird in your flock and save them all, then lose the next batch of chicks you add because the soil is still loaded with oocysts. Treating the birds is only half the job.

Here's the practical cleanup sequence:

1. **Remove and dispose of all litter.** Don't compost it until it's fully broken down. Oocysts can survive standard composting temperatures. Hot composting above 130°F for several weeks kills them.

2. **Scrub feeders and waterers** with a dilute bleach solution (1 tablespoon bleach per gallon of water), rinse well, and let them dry in sunlight. UV light helps destroy oocysts.

3. **Dry the coop out completely.** Coccidiosis thrives in moisture. Improve ventilation. Fix roof and wall leaks. If your run stays wet, add coarse sand to improve drainage.

4. **Treat the soil if the run is small.** Agricultural lime won't kill oocysts directly, but it raises soil pH and dries out the environment, making it inhospitable. Some people use Coop Refresher or similar products.

5. **Don't put new or young birds on infected ground** until the run has dried out and you've finished the environmental cleanup. Even then, consider using medicated chick starter for the first 6 to 8 weeks.

Here's the honest truth: you probably can't prevent exposure, and you probably shouldn't try.

What you want is a flock that builds immunity through controlled low-level exposure. Not one that encounters *Eimeria* for the first time as stressed adults.

**Medicated chick starter** contains low-dose amprolium that slows oocyst development enough to let chicks build immunity while preventing overwhelming infection. It's not a cure and not foolproof, but it significantly reduces outbreak risk during the highest-vulnerability window. If you're buying vaccinated chicks (Marek's included), check whether the vaccine covers coccidiosis, because some do. Vaccinated chicks shouldn't get medicated feed, as amprolium interferes with the live coccidiosis vaccine.

**Keep bedding dry.** This is probably the single most effective preventive step you can take. A nipple waterer instead of an open trough cuts spillage dramatically. **Don't overcrowd.** Overcrowding increases oocyst concentration in the environment and suppresses immune function through stress. The recommended minimums (4 square feet per bird inside, 10 square feet in the run) exist for a reason.

**Quarantine new birds.** Always. A minimum of 30 days in completely separate space before integration. A bird from another flock may carry *Eimeria* strains your flock has zero immunity to.

---

Coccidiosis is one of those things every chicken keeper eventually faces. It doesn't mean you failed or that your setup is wrong. It means you're keeping chickens, which means keeping animals that live on the ground and share space with a parasite that lives in almost every soil. What separates the keepers who lose whole flocks from the ones who catch it and move on is knowing what to look for and having Corid on hand before you need it. Keep a bottle in your coop kit. Know your symptoms. And if something feels off when you open the coop door in the morning, trust that feeling.

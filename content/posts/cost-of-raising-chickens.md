Nobody warned me that my "cheap" backyard egg setup would cost me $1,400 before a single hen laid her first egg. I'd read all the beginner guides. I bought the $89 starter coop from Tractor Supply, grabbed six chicks at $4.99 each from the local feed store, and figured I was being smart and frugal. I was not.

The real cost of raising chickens is one of the most consistently undersold topics in the hobby. People love to talk about the $0.30 egg math. Nobody wants to talk about the $600 predator-proof hardware cloth retrofit they had to do after losing their first flock to a raccoon that ripped through the cheap wire stapled to that starter coop. I've had both experiences. Let me give you the actual numbers.

## Startup Costs: Where Most People Underestimate

The coop is the first place the budget goes sideways. Here's the honest breakdown: a coop suitable for four to six hens, one that doesn't need major rework within a year, is going to run you between $300 and $800 if you buy pre-built. The $89 Tractor Supply coops and the similar ones on Amazon (you'll see them for $120-$250) are, I'll be honest, not adequate for serious keeping. The latches are flimsy. The ventilation is terrible. The floor gaps are wide enough for weasels. I know this because I used one.

If you're handy, a DIY coop built from dimensional lumber, OSB for the walls, and hardware cloth (not chicken wire, never chicken wire) runs roughly $200-$450 in materials depending on your region and how far you're willing to go on features. The hardware cloth alone is significant: a roll of 1/2-inch galvanized hardware cloth from Home Depot runs about $55-$75 for a 25-foot by 2-foot roll, and you'll use more than one.

Then there's the run. A covered, predator-resistant run adds another $150-$400 depending on size. Apron wire around the base, buried six inches out, is non-negotiable if you have raccoons, skunks, or foxes nearby (and you almost certainly do, even in the suburbs).

The full startup picture for a small flock of four to six hens, being realistic:

- Coop (DIY moderate quality): $300-$450
- Run construction: $150-$350
- Hardware cloth for coop and run (predator-proofing): $100-$200
- Chicks (six pullets from a hatchery or feed store): $30-$90 depending on breed
- Brooder setup (heat lamp, brooder box, bedding): $40-$80
- Feeder and waterer: $25-$60
- Initial feed, grit, and electrolytes: $40-$60
- Nesting box pads or straw: $15-$30

You're looking at $700 to $1,320 before your birds are even in the coop. What surprised me was how fast the hardware adds up compared to the birds themselves. The chickens are almost the cheapest part.

## The Ongoing Cost Nobody Bothers to Calculate

Feed is the biggest monthly expense, full stop. A 50-pound bag of quality layer pellets (I use Purina Layena or Kalmbach Feeds, both around $22-$28 per bag depending on your area) lasts four to six standard-size hens about a month if you're supplementing with scraps and they have some range time. Six birds with no ranging? You might burn through a bag in three weeks.

So call it $25-$35 per month for feed for a small flock. That's $300-$420 per year, every year.

Then there's bedding. If you're using the deep litter method in a walk-in coop, a bale of pine shavings (Tractor Supply's small animal pine shavings, about $7-$9 for a compressed bale) every two to three weeks is a reasonable estimate. If you're doing a smaller coop with more frequent cleaning, you might go through more. Budget $8-$12 per month, $96-$144 per year.

Scratch grains, oyster shell for calcium, occasional treats. Another $5-$10 per month on average if you're doing this right. And grit, if your birds don't have access to natural ground. Not a big expense, a $10 bag lasts a small flock several months, but it's another thing people forget to budget.

Healthcare costs are where people get genuinely blindsided. The research here is mixed on how often backyard flocks need veterinary attention, but in my experience, expect at least one significant health situation per year across a flock of six. Respiratory infections, wry neck from vitamin deficiency, bumblefoot, egg binding. A vet visit for a chicken runs $50-$150 at an avian vet, and not every town has one within a reasonable drive. I've learned to treat a lot of things myself. Vetericyn Poultry Care spray ($14 on Amazon) lives in my barn. Tylan Soluble powder for respiratory infections, around $18, has probably paid for itself ten times over. But there will be losses. There will be a hen you didn't expect to lose. That's part of this.

A realistic monthly operating cost for six laying hens: $38-$60 per month, or roughly $450-$720 per year.

## The Real Egg Math

Here's where it gets uncomfortable. The average layer hen produces around 250-300 eggs per year in her peak years, dropping off noticeably after year two. Six hens at peak might give you 4-5 dozen eggs per week in summer, and maybe 1-2 dozen per week in the dead of winter without supplemental lighting (and even with lighting, production drops).

Over a year, six hens might produce 100-130 dozen eggs. At current grocery store prices, even the cheap eggs at $3.50-$4.00 per dozen, that's $350-$520 in egg value. Organic free-range eggs at $7-$8 per dozen gets you closer to $700-$1,040.

Your annual operating cost is $450-$720. Your first-year cost including startup amortized over, say, four years (optimistic hen lifespan for production purposes) adds another $250-$330 per year. So your total annual cost is somewhere between $700 and $1,050 in a realistic scenario.

The math only works if you value your eggs at the premium price point and your flock performs well. I'll be honest: most backyard setups do not "pay for themselves" in any reasonable timeframe if you're counting labor, even at minimum wage. What they do provide is eggs you know the provenance of, a connection to your food, genuine entertainment value, and a hobby that's genuinely absorbing. If that's worth it to you, the cost is justified. It was for me.

## Where You Can Save (And Where You Shouldn't)

Skimping on coop construction and predator protection is the most expensive mistake in this hobby. I have friends who've rebuilt or significantly upgraded their first coops. Every single one says the same thing: they wish they'd just done it right the first time. Do not use chicken wire. Do not use a coop with a pull-ring latch (racoons open them). Do not skip the apron wire. A $200 predator-proofing investment prevents a $0 flock.

Feed quality is a place where I'd argue against going too cheap, but the difference between a $22 bag of Purina Layena and the cheapest bulk layer pellet at your co-op isn't going to be dramatic. What matters more is freshness and whether the feed has been stored properly. Old, moldy feed is a health problem.

Where you genuinely can save:

**Breed selection.** A production red or ISA Brown costs $4-$6 as a chick and lays 280-300 eggs per year. An ornamental or rare breed might cost $20-$60 as a pullet and lay 150-200 eggs. Know what you're buying. I love my Easter Eggers for the egg color, but they're not production machines. If eggs are the primary goal, stick with sex-linked production breeds.

**Free ranging.** Even two to three hours of daily ranging reduces feed consumption noticeably and gives you better yolk color without any supplement. This requires a secure perimeter or a watchful eye, but the savings are real. I've estimated 15-20% feed reduction during heavy-ranging months.

**DIY feeders and waterers.** A 5-gallon bucket nipple waterer costs maybe $12 in parts and lasts indefinitely. A decent hanging feeder from Premier 1 or Little Giant runs $20-$35 and lasts for years. These are worth buying quality once. (Note: this site may earn a commission on Amazon purchases.)

What surprised me most after ten years is how the costs become almost invisible once you're past the startup phase. The $25 bag of feed is just part of the month. The occasional vet expense is annoying but manageable. The startup costs are real and front-loaded, which is why the first year always feels expensive.

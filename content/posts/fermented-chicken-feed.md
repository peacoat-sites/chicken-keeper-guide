Fermented feed cut my flock's feed consumption by roughly 20% within the first three weeks. I wasn't expecting that. I started fermenting mostly because a reader named Jess from rural Wisconsin emailed me asking whether it was worth the hassle, and I realized I'd been half-doing it for years without ever tracking anything carefully. So I actually paid attention this time.

Here's what you need to know upfront: fermentation isn't magic, it's microbiology. What you're doing is creating a lacto-fermented feed using naturally occurring Lactobacillus bacteria, the same process behind sauerkraut and sourdough. The bacteria pre-digest some of the starches and phytic acid in the grain, which improves nutrient bioavailability and creates beneficial probiotics. A 2012 study published in Poultry Science found that hens fed lacto-fermented feed showed significantly improved feed conversion ratios and higher [egg production](/adding-light-for-winter-egg-production/) compared to controls fed the same dry ration. The mechanism is fairly well understood: fermentation reduces phytic acid (a compound that blocks mineral absorption) by up to 60%, according to research from Wageningen University, which means your birds are actually absorbing more of what they eat.

What surprised me was how hard this is to find clearly written up. Most sources either treat it like a mystical folk practice or oversimplify it to the point of uselessness.

## What Actually Happens During Fermentation

You're not doing anything fancy. You're soaking feed in water and letting wild Lactobacillus bacteria (already present on the grain) multiply under anaerobic conditions. The bacteria produce lactic acid, which drops the pH of the feed and inhibits harmful bacteria like Salmonella and E. coli. A 2014 paper from the University of Copenhagen confirmed that lacto-fermented feed showed significantly reduced Campylobacter colonization in broiler flocks, a finding that's held up in a few follow-on studies.

The key word is anaerobic. The feed needs to be submerged under water so that oxygen-dependent (aerobic) bacteria can't dominate. This is why a weight on top of your fermenting bucket matters more than most guides admit. I learned this the hard way my first season when I kept getting a thin white film on top of the feed. That's kahm yeast, not dangerous, but it means your feed wasn't fully submerged and aerobic organisms got a foothold. Press the feed down, weight it, and keep it covered. Problem solved.

Temperature matters. Fermentation happens fastest around 70-75°F. In my basement in January it sometimes takes closer to 5 days. In August in a non-insulated garage, I've had batches turn sour and over-fermented in under 48 hours. The smell you're looking for is pleasantly sour, like yogurt or sourdough. If it smells rotten, genuinely putrid, not just tangy, toss it.

## The Numbers: Cost, Consumption, and What to Expect

I'll be honest: the cost savings aren't enormous on a small flock, but they're real and they compound. My six Australorps were going through about 1.75 lbs of layer pellets [per day](/how-much-to-feed-chickens-per-day/) collective. On fermented feed, that dropped to around 1.4 lbs. At current prices (as of July 2026, a 50 lb bag of Purina Layena runs about $23-$26 depending on your supplier), that's roughly $4-5 saved per month on feed alone for six birds. Not life-changing, but over a year, you're looking at $50-$60, and the health benefits are harder to price.

The fermented whole grain number surprised me too. Whole grains ferment more completely than processed pellets because there's more intact starch for the bacteria to work with. That said, if your birds aren't used to whole grain rations, don't switch cold turkey. Mix it in over two weeks or you'll see a temporary drop in production.

## How to Actually Do It

No special equipment. Here's what I use: two 5-gallon food-grade buckets (around $4 each at a hardware store, or free from a bakery), a small plate or piece of wood to weight the feed down, and a loose-fitting lid or towel to cover without sealing airtight.

Day 1: Fill a bucket about 1/3 full with dry feed. Cover with dechlorinated water (let tap water sit for an hour or use filtered) to at least 2-3 inches above the feed. Grain absorbs a lot. Stir it, weight it down, cover loosely. Leave at room temperature.

Day 2: Stir again. It'll look slimy and might smell faintly sour already. Good. Top off water if the grain has absorbed above the waterline. Weight it back down.

Day 3: It should smell noticeably tangy and you'll see tiny bubbles if you disturb it. That's CO2 from active fermentation. Taste a little if you're brave. It's pleasantly sour.

Day 3-4: Start feeding. Scoop out what you need (a wet slurry at this point), then immediately add more dry feed and water to the bucket to keep the batch going. This is your perpetual starter. The residual liquid, cloudy with Lactobacillus, seeds the new batch and cuts future fermentation time to 24-36 hours.

The biggest practical thing I'd tell you: don't make more than your flock can eat in one feeding session. Fermented feed left in feeders on a July afternoon turns rank within a few hours and can cause sour crop if birds eat enough of it. I found this out after leaving a too-generous scoop out on an 89-degree afternoon. One hen had a squishy, off-smelling crop that evening. She recovered after I withheld feed overnight and gave her some [apple cider vinegar](/apple-cider-vinegar-for-chickens/) water, but it was a reminder that this stuff behaves like fresh food, not shelf-stable pellets.

## Which Feed to Ferment

Not everything ferments equally well. Layer pellets work fine but they turn into mush fast and lose their shape within about 24 hours. Crumbles are worse, basically dissolving into a paste. Whole grains (scratch grains, whole wheat, whole oats, barley) hold their texture better and give birds something to work with, which they seem to prefer. Fermented grains also have a more complete fermentation because the hull and endosperm are intact.

I've had good luck with Scratch and Peck Feeds Cluckin' Good Layer mash (around $45 for 25 lbs, which sounds steep until you account for the consumption reduction). It's designed to be fermented, the texture holds reasonably well, and the company actually publishes fermentation instructions on their packaging. That's not an endorsement, just an observation that some feeds are genuinely designed for this.

One thing to avoid: medicated chick starter. The amprolium in it can be degraded or its effectiveness altered by fermentation, and the research on exactly what happens is mixed enough that I wouldn't risk it with young birds.

## Does It Actually Improve Egg Production?

I'll be honest here: the research is genuinely mixed. The 2012 Poultry Science study showed improvements, a few other controlled trials have replicated modest gains, but the effect size varies a lot by breed, age of the flock, and baseline diet quality. My Australorps went from averaging 4-5 eggs per 6 birds per day to 5-6 per day in the month after I switched. Is that fermentation, or was it a seasonal thing (I switched in April, right as day length was increasing)? I genuinely don't know.

What I do feel more confident about is gut health. Flock-wide loose droppings dropped off noticeably. Two of my older hens that had intermittent soft stool issues for most of a year cleared up within about six weeks on fermented feed. Anecdote, obviously, but consistent with what the Lactobacillus literature would predict.

### FAQ

#### How long does fermented chicken feed last once it's ready?

Once fermented and removed from the starter bucket, serve it the same day. Don't leave uneaten portions out for more than 2 hours in warm weather. The batch in the bucket itself stays active for 4-7 days if you keep adding dry feed and water to it.

#### Can I ferment medicated chick starter?

I'd avoid it. Amprolium, the coccidiostat in most medicated starters, may be altered by the fermentation process, and there isn't enough controlled data to know if it remains effective. Use plain (non-medicated) chick starter or switch to fermented feed after chicks no longer need medication.

#### What does bad fermented feed smell like vs. good?

Good fermented feed smells tangy and sour, similar to yogurt or sourdough bread. Bad fermented feed smells genuinely rotten, sulfuric, or like ammonia. If you're unsure, it's not worth risking it. Toss it and start fresh.

#### Will fermented feed cause sour crop?

Sour crop is caused by Candida overgrowth in the crop, often from eating spoiled or moldy feed. Properly fermented feed doesn't cause it, but leaving fermented feed out too long in heat allows secondary harmful organisms to grow, and that can be a trigger. Keep portions small and remove leftovers promptly.

#### Do I need to change anything else about my feeding setup?

Yes, practically speaking. Standard dry feeders don't work with fermented slurry. I use a wide, shallow rubber feed pan (about $6 from a farm supply store), one per 3-4 birds. Clean it between feedings or bacteria and mold from leftover residue can contaminate fresh batches.

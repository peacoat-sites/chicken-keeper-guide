Most coverage of H5N1 and backyard flocks focuses on your chickens dying. That's real, but it's only half the story. On June 25, 2026, a University of Queensland veterinary expert published a warning that cuts closer to home: H5N1 can infect and kill your cat. Then in May 2026, the CDC documented the first case of a human contracting H5N1 through exposure to an infected domestic cat. The transmission chain now reads: wild bird to backyard flock to cat to person. If you keep chickens and you have a cat, you are sitting at the center of that chain.

## The Cat Problem Nobody's Talking About

Cats are obligate hunters. They eat birds. They investigate dead things. If H5N1 moves through your flock, your cat is going to be curious about exactly the wrong thing at exactly the wrong time.

The American Veterinary Medical Association has tracked more than 100 cats that became severely ill or died from H5N1 since the cattle outbreak began in the United States. That number almost certainly undercounts reality, because most backyard cats don't get necropsied when they die mysteriously. The clinical picture in cats is bad: neurological signs, respiratory distress, rapid deterioration. Many don't survive to a second vet visit.

The University of Queensland expert described H5N1 as having "caused havoc in poultry farms and backyard chicken flocks in the northern hemisphere," and specifically flagged cats as a vulnerable species that owners consistently underestimate as a risk factor. The concern isn't theoretical. It's documented, it's recent, and it's accelerating.

## How the Virus Gets Into Your Yard

You don't need a commercial poultry operation next door. Urban and suburban bridging species, crows and magpies specifically, can carry H5N1 into your yard and infect your flock without you ever seeing a sick wild bird. The bird may look fine. It sheds virus. Your hens investigate the ground where it landed.

From there the timeline compresses fast. World Organisation for Animal Health data shows that following H5N1 detection in wild birds in an area, poultry outbreaks typically follow within two to six weeks. That's your biosecurity window, and it's short. By the time you hear about wild bird detections in your county, the clock is already running.

Your cat then walks through the same yard, sniffs the same soil, finds a dead hen you missed near the fence line, or catches an infected wild bird that came down sick. Cats can also be exposed through raw chicken parts, including eggs from infected flocks, which some backyard keepers handle casually and set down on surfaces where cats roam.

A CDC survey published in May 2026 found that 71% of backyard flock owners had no veterinarian they could consult about their birds, and one-third didn't know the signs of avian flu in either birds or people. Read that again: one in three keepers couldn't recognize the disease in their own flock or themselves.

The signs in chickens aren't subtle once the virus is active. Sudden high mortality, neurological symptoms like twisted necks and loss of coordination, swollen wattles and combs, respiratory distress, and birds that stop eating abruptly. If you're seeing three or more birds sick simultaneously with no obvious cause, that's your signal to act fast, not wait and see.

The human case documented in May 2026 involved a veterinary professional with serologic evidence of H5N1 infection after exposure to a domestic cat that had tested positive. That's a cat-to-human transmission event. It didn't require handling infected poultry directly. It required proximity to a sick cat.

## The Practical Biosecurity Adjustments That Actually Matter

You don't need to tear down your coop. You need to close specific gaps that most backyard setups leave open.

Keep cats out of the chicken run. This sounds obvious until you realize most backyard setups have gaps a cat can squeeze through, and cats often den under coops where bedding, feathers, and fecal matter accumulate. That's a high-concentration exposure zone. Block it.

Stop free-ranging if wild bird activity in your area is elevated. Free-ranging hens are beautiful in theory. Right now, in the summer of 2026, letting birds roam in areas frequented by crows and waterfowl is a genuine risk calculation you should be making consciously, not by default.

Remove carcasses immediately. Don't let a dead bird sit in the yard for hours while you finish work. Bag it, glove up, wash hands. This also applies to wild birds that die near your property. Don't let your cat near them.

Covered runs aren't just predator protection. Wire overhead keeps wild birds from landing in the run and defecating into feed and water. If your run isn't covered, that's your most important infrastructure upgrade right now.

Find a vet. Seventy-one percent of flock owners have no avian vet contact. You don't need to see one proactively, but you need the number before you need it. Many states have agricultural extension services with poultry health contacts. The AVMA's [avian influenza resources](https://www.avma.org/resources-tools/animal-health-and-welfare/animal-health/avian-influenza/avian-influenza-companion-animals) include guidance on companion animal exposure. Use them now, not after you've got sick birds.

## When to Report and Who to Call

If birds in your flock die suddenly and in numbers, contact your state veterinarian or the USDA APHIS Veterinary Services line. Don't wait for certainty. Reporting suspected cases doesn't automatically mean your flock gets culled. It does mean you get answers faster.

If your cat has had access to sick or dead birds and begins showing neurological signs or respiratory distress, call your vet immediately and disclose the exposure history. The May 2026 CDC case demonstrates that this is no longer a "could theoretically happen" scenario.

Most backyard keepers got into this for the eggs, the connection to food, and the pleasure of keeping animals well. None of that changes. What changes is that the disease landscape in 2026 requires treating H5N1 as a whole-household risk, not just a flock problem. Your chickens, your cat, and you are in the same transmission network. Act like it.

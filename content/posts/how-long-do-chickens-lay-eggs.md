A hen that laid an egg every single day for her first two years can suddenly drop to just a handful of eggs per month, and many backyard flock owners are completely blindsided when it happens. Understanding *why* that shift occurs, and how long you can realistically expect your hens to produce, saves you from frustration, helps you plan your flock rotation, and honestly makes you a better chicken keeper overall.

## The Egg-Laying Lifespan: What to Realistically Expect

Chickens don't lay eggs forever. A hen is born with every egg follicle she'll ever have, roughly 2,000 potential eggs packed into her ovaries from day one. That's the ceiling. She'll never produce more than that number, and most hens never get anywhere close to it.

Here's how the typical laying lifespan breaks down:

**Year 1 (Peak Production)**
A pullet usually starts laying somewhere between 18 and 24 weeks of age, depending on breed. Her first eggs are often small and irregular, but within a month or two she hits her stride. During this first laying year, a high-producing breed like a Leghorn or an ISA Brown can average 280 to 300 eggs per year. That's nearly an egg a day.

**Year 2 (Still Strong, Slight Dip)**
Production typically drops about 10 to 15 percent in the second year. You probably won't notice much difference if you've got a mixed-age flock. Eggs also tend to get larger in year two, which is a nice trade-off.

**Years 3 to 4 (Noticeable Decline)**
By year three, many hens are producing 50 to 60 percent of their year-one output. A hen who was giving you 5 to 6 eggs a week is now down to 3 or maybe 4. Some keepers find this totally acceptable, especially for pet hens.

**Year 5 and Beyond (Occasional Layers)**
Most hens become sporadic layers past five years. Some will surprise you with a few eggs per month well into their seventh or eighth year, but it's the exception rather than the rule. Hens can live 8 to 10 years, and many backyard keepers happily keep aging hens for their personality, pest control, and entertainment value long after the eggs stop.

## Why Do Hens Stop Laying?

The drop-off isn't random. Several specific factors trigger reduced production or cause it to stop altogether.

**Molting**
Every fall, hens go through a molt: they shed their old feathers and grow new ones. The process takes 8 to 16 weeks, and during that time most hens stop laying entirely. The body redirects protein from egg production to feather regrowth. Once the molt is done and day length stabilizes, laying resumes.

**Shortened Daylight**
Hens need 14 to 16 hours of light per day to maintain peak laying. As days shorten in fall and winter, the pineal gland registers fewer light hours and signals the reproductive system to slow down. This is why production tanks in November and picks back up in February or March, often with no other changes to management. Many keepers add a simple 40-watt bulb on a timer in the coop to maintain artificial day length through winter.

**Age and Depleted Follicles**
High-production commercial hybrids burn through follicles faster because they're engineered to lay almost every day. Heritage breeds lay less frequently, which means their follicle supply lasts longer. This is one reason a Dominique or a Buckeye might still give you occasional eggs at age six, while a production Red might be done by four.

**Stress**
A hen under stress stops laying. Heat above 95 degrees Fahrenheit, a predator attack, a sudden change in flock dynamics, overcrowding, or a disruption in routine can all tank production. I've seen a flock drop to almost nothing for two weeks after a raccoon got into the coop at night, even though no birds were physically harmed. The fear alone was enough.

**Nutrition Deficiencies**
A hen producing an egg every day or two needs substantial calcium (for the shell), protein (for the white and yolk), and a reliable calorie supply. Low-quality feed, or a diet heavy in scratch and treats with not enough layer pellets, can cause laying to slow or stop. Our [chicken feed types explained](/chicken-feed-types-explained/) guide covers what your hens actually need nutritionally at each life stage.

**Illness or Parasites**
Reproductive infections, egg yolk peritonitis, lice, mites, and internal parasites all affect production. If your hens' decline is sudden and accompanied by other symptoms, a vet check is worth the time.

## Breed Makes a Bigger Difference Than Most People Realize

Not all hens are built the same. Production breeds like Leghorns, Golden Comets, and ISA Browns are engineered for maximum output and will outlay almost any heritage breed in years one and two. But that intensity comes at a cost: they tend to age out of productive laying faster.

Heritage breeds, dual-purpose birds, and some of the calmer docile breeds lay fewer eggs per year but distribute that output over more years. A Plymouth Rock, for example, might average 200 eggs per year but hold around 150 eggs per year through age five. Compared to a production hybrid that gave you 300 in year one and 80 in year four, the Rock's consistency has real value.

## How to Maximize the Productive Years Your Hens Have

You can't stop the biological clock, but you can absolutely extend how long your hens lay at a decent rate. Here's what actually moves the needle:

**1. Feed layer pellets as the base diet (not scratch)**
Layer feed should make up at least 80 to 90 percent of your hens' diet. Scratch is a treat, not a staple. Offer a separate dish of oyster shell free-choice so laying hens can regulate their own calcium intake.

**2. Keep water fresh and always available**
A hen who runs out of water for even a few hours can pause her laying cycle. In hot weather, that can mean days of disruption. A [reliable watering system](/chicken-water-requirements/) is genuinely one of the highest-return investments you can make.

**3. Manage light in winter**
Adding a simple light on a timer set to come on before dawn (so total light exposure hits 15 hours) extends your winter production without stressing the birds. Just know that choosing to let hens molt and rest in winter has its own long-term benefits for laying longevity.

**4. Control stress in the coop**
Overcrowding is the number one source of flock stress. Give laying hens at least 4 square feet of indoor space per bird. Keep the coop well-ventilated but draft-free. Stable flock dynamics matter too: adding new birds mid-season disrupts everyone's production.

**5. Refresh your flock every 2 to 3 years**
If you want a consistent egg supply without keeping a massive flock, the simplest strategy is staggered additions. Add 4 to 6 new pullets every two or three years so you always have birds in peak production while your older hens wind down gracefully.

**6. Protect against parasites proactively**
Check your birds for lice and mites monthly. A good dusting area with wood ash or food-grade diatomaceous earth helps. Internal parasites are trickier but worth monitoring, especially in older hens.

## Signs Your Hen Is Slowing Down (Versus Something Being Wrong)

There's a difference between a normal, age-related decline and a problem that needs attention. Knowing the difference keeps you from missing a real health issue.

Normal signs of age-related decline:
- Gradual reduction in eggs over several months
- Larger eggs with thinner shells (common in older hens)
- Slower molt recovery in fall
- Less frequent squatting behavior

Signs that something else might be going on:
- Sudden stop in laying in a bird under 3 years old
- Weight loss, pale comb, or lethargy alongside fewer eggs
- Swollen abdomen (can indicate internal laying or egg yolk peritonitis)
- Eggs with no shells, wrinkled shells, or double yolks regularly
- Other flock members showing similar symptoms at the same time

If you're seeing the second set of signs, don't just wait it out. A quick vet visit or at minimum a call to a poultry extension line is the right move.

Egg production is a long game. The hens that gave you a basketful every week in their first two years earned a little grace as they age, and the ones you add to the flock this year are already quietly ticking through their finite supply of follicles. Plan ahead, rotate your flock thoughtfully, keep the basics locked in, and you'll almost always have enough eggs. The hens who've slowed down? They're still worth keeping around.

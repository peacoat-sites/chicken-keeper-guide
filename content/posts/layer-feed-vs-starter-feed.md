You picked up a bag of feed at the farm store, tossed it in the car, and drove home feeling like you had this chicken thing figured out. Then you get home and the label says "Starter" but your hens are 22 weeks old and laying their first eggs. Does it actually matter? Yes. Feeding the wrong formula at the wrong life stage can mean soft-shelled eggs, bone problems, stunted pullets, or kidney damage in young chicks. This isn't marketing fluff. It's nutrition science that directly affects your flock's health and how many eggs you'll get.

## What Makes Starter Feed Different

Starter feed is for chicks from hatch through roughly 6-8 weeks. The key feature is high protein, typically between 18% and 22% depending on the brand. That protein supports the explosive growth happening in those first weeks when a chick is building its skeleton, organs, and feathers from nothing.

Starter feed also has a carefully balanced amino acid profile, particularly methionine and lysine, which are critical for muscle and feather development. You'll find most starters in two forms: medicated and non-medicated. The medicated version contains amprolium, a coccidiostat that protects chicks from coccidiosis, a parasitic intestinal disease that can wipe out young birds fast. If your chicks were vaccinated for coccidiosis at the hatchery, skip the medicated version. Combining vaccination and amprolium can actually reduce immunity effectiveness.

Some keepers use a "starter-grower" blend that bridges 0 to 18 weeks instead. These typically run 16-18% protein and save you from buying two separate products. They won't be quite as protein-rich as a dedicated starter, but they're perfectly adequate for most backyard flocks. ## What Layer Feed Actually Does

Layer feed is designed for hens actively producing eggs, generally from 18-20 weeks onward. Protein drops compared to starter, settling around 15-17%. Calcium jumps up dramatically. Layer feed contains roughly 3.5-4.5% calcium, compared to about 1% in starter feed.

That calcium isn't optional. Each egg your hen produces requires about 2 grams of calcium just for the shell. A laying hen needs around 4 grams of total calcium per day to produce one egg and maintain her own bone density. Without that dietary calcium, her body pulls it directly from her bones. Do that for a few months and you get hens suffering from osteoporosis, fractures, and "cage layer fatigue," which happens in backyard flocks too. It's not just a factory farm problem.

Layer feed also typically includes vitamin D3 to help with calcium absorption, along with phosphorus, manganese, and zinc to support overall reproductive health. Some brands add omega-3 sources like flaxseed, which means eggs with better yolk nutrition. ## Why Feeding the Wrong Feed Is a Real Problem

This is where it gets serious. The high calcium in layer feed is appropriate for an adult laying hen, but it's genuinely harmful to young chicks and pullets whose kidneys aren't equipped to handle that mineral load. Excess calcium in birds under 18 weeks has been linked to kidney damage and gout. Backyard chicken keepers have lost young birds to renal failure after feeding layer pellets too early. These aren't theoretical risks.

Going the other direction has its own problems. If you're feeding starter to a laying hen, she's getting almost no dietary calcium. You might crack open eggs and find paper-thin shells, or she'll start laying shell-less eggs. Internally, she's depleting her own skeletal calcium, which leads to egg binding as her muscles weaken and to long-term bone fragility.

There's also a flock management reality that trips up a lot of people. If you're raising chicks and adults together, or if you have pullets transitioning to lay at different times, feeding one universal ration becomes tricky. The practical solution many experienced keepers use is to offer an all-flock or flock raiser feed (typically 18-20% protein) to everyone, and then provide free-choice oyster shell on the side. The laying hens will self-regulate and eat the oyster shell as they need it. Young birds generally won't bother with it. I've run mixed flocks this way for years and it works consistently well.

## Starter to Layer: How and When to Transition

Timing the transition matters. Most commercial layer feed companies recommend switching at 18 weeks, but there's a more accurate indicator: the onset of laying. Once you see your first eggs, those pullets are ready for layer feed. If your flock is mixed-age and some birds are laying at 18 weeks while others aren't until 22 weeks, that's another good argument for the all-flock plus oyster shell approach during the transition window.

Here's a practical timeline:

**Weeks 0-6:** Chick starter, 18-22% protein. Medicated if chicks weren't vaccinated for coccidiosis.

**Weeks 6-18:** Grower or starter-grower, 16-18% protein. Reduce medicated feed at this stage, and many keepers simply move to non-medicated grower.

**Week 18 or first egg:** Begin transitioning to layer feed. Don't switch overnight. Mix the feeds over about 7-10 days to avoid digestive upset.

**Ongoing:** Layer feed with 15-17% protein and 3.5-4.5% calcium. Add free-choice oyster shell alongside the feeder regardless. Some hens need more calcium than the feed provides, especially heavy producers.

This applies to standard breeds. If you're raising meat-heavy dual-purpose breeds or high-production layers, protein requirements can differ slightly. [Dual purpose chicken breeds](/dual-purpose-chicken-breeds/) sometimes benefit from staying on a higher-protein grower feed a bit longer given their heavier frame development.

## Choosing the Right Feed Product

Not all feeds are created equal. Here are things to actually look at on the label before buying:

**Protein source:** Soy-based feeds are the most common and least expensive. If you prefer soy-free options (some keepers worry about phytoestrogens), expect to pay more. Organic and non-GMO feeds exist in both categories.

**Feed form:** Crumbles tend to work better for younger birds, while pellets reduce waste for adults. Mash is the least processed but also the messiest. Wasted feed means wasted money.

**Calcium-to-phosphorus ratio:** You want roughly a 2:1 ratio in layer feed. Too much phosphorus relative to calcium can interfere with absorption.

**Additives:** Some feeds include probiotics, prebiotics, or marigold extract for yolk color. These aren't gimmicks. Marigold meal genuinely deepens yolk color, and probiotics can support gut health especially after stress or antibiotic treatment.

Getting the feed right isn't complicated once you understand what each formula is actually doing. Starter feed builds a bird. Layer feed sustains production without depleting the bird. Keep those two jobs in mind, time your transitions around real biological markers instead of arbitrary dates, and your flock will reward you with consistent eggs and long healthy lives.

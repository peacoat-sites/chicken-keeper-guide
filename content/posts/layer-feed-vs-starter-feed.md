Imagine you've just picked up a bag of feed at the farm store, tossed it in the car, and driven home feeling like a responsible chicken keeper. Then you get home and realize the bag says "Starter" but your hens are 22 weeks old and laying their first eggs. Does it matter? Yes, quite a lot, actually. Feeding the wrong formula at the wrong life stage can mean soft-shelled eggs, bone problems, stunted pullets, or kidney damage in young chicks. The difference between layer feed and starter feed isn't just marketing. It's nutrition science that directly affects your flock's health and productivity.

## What Makes Starter Feed Different

Starter feed is formulated for chicks from hatch through roughly 6-8 weeks of age. The defining feature is high protein, typically between 18% and 22% depending on the brand. That protein content supports the explosive growth happening in those first weeks when a chick is essentially building its skeleton, organs, and feathers from scratch.

Starter feed also contains a carefully balanced amino acid profile, particularly methionine and lysine, which are critical for muscle and feather development. You'll find most starters are available in two forms: medicated and non-medicated. The medicated version contains amprolium, a coccidiostat that helps protect chicks from coccidiosis, a parasitic intestinal disease that can wipe out young birds fast. If your chicks were vaccinated for coccidiosis at the hatchery, skip the medicated version, since combining vaccination and amprolium can actually reduce immunity effectiveness.

Some keepers also use a "starter-grower" blend that bridges the gap from 0 to 18 weeks. These typically run 16-18% protein and are a convenient option if you don't want to buy two separate products. They won't be quite as protein-rich as a dedicated starter, but they're perfectly adequate for most backyard flocks. For a deeper look at the full range of options on the market, our guide to [chicken feed types explained](/chicken-feed-types-explained/) breaks everything down by life stage and purpose.

## What Layer Feed Actually Does

Layer feed is designed for hens that are actively producing eggs, generally from 18-20 weeks onward. The protein level drops compared to starter, settling in around 15-17%. What jumps up dramatically is calcium. Layer feed contains roughly 3.5-4.5% calcium, compared to about 1% in starter feed.

That calcium isn't optional. Each egg your hen produces requires about 2 grams of calcium just for the shell. A laying hen needs around 4 grams of total calcium per day to produce one egg and maintain her own bone density. Without that dietary calcium, her body will pull it directly from her bones. Do that for a few months and you end up with hens suffering from osteoporosis, fractures, and a condition called "cage layer fatigue," which can occur in backyard hens too. It's not just a factory farm problem.

Layer feed also typically includes vitamin D3 to help with calcium absorption, along with phosphorus, manganese, and zinc to support overall reproductive health. Some brands add omega-3 sources like flaxseed, which translates into eggs with better yolk nutrition. ## Why Feeding the Wrong Feed Is a Real Problem

This is where it gets serious. The high calcium in layer feed is appropriate for an adult laying hen, but it's genuinely harmful to young chicks and pullets whose kidneys aren't equipped to handle that mineral load. Excess calcium in birds under 18 weeks has been linked to kidney damage and gout. These aren't theoretical risks. Backyard chicken keepers have lost young birds to renal failure after feeding layer pellets too early.

Going the other direction has its own issues. If you're feeding starter to a laying hen, she's getting almost no dietary calcium. You might crack open eggs and find paper-thin shells, or she'll start laying shell-less eggs entirely. Internally, she's depleting her own skeletal calcium, which can lead to egg binding as her muscles weaken and to long-term bone fragility.

There's also a flock management reality that trips up a lot of people. If you're raising chicks and adults together, or if you have pullets transitioning to lay at different times, feeding one universal ration becomes tricky. The practical solution many experienced keepers use is to offer an all-flock or flock raiser feed (typically 18-20% protein) to everyone, and then provide free-choice oyster shell on the side. The laying hens will self-regulate and eat the oyster shell as they need it. Young birds generally won't bother with it. I've run mixed flocks this way for years and it works consistently well.

## Starter to Layer: How and When to Transition

Timing the transition matters. Most commercial layer feed companies recommend switching at 18 weeks, but there's a more accurate indicator: the onset of laying. Once you see your first eggs, those pullets are ready for layer feed. If your flock is mixed-age and some birds are laying at 18 weeks while others aren't until 22 weeks, that's another good argument for the all-flock plus oyster shell approach during the transition window.

Here's a practical transition timeline you can actually follow:

**Weeks 0-6:** Chick starter, 18-22% protein. Medicated if chicks weren't vaccinated for coccidiosis.

**Weeks 6-18:** Grower or starter-grower, 16-18% protein. Reduce medicated feed at this stage, and many keepers simply move to non-medicated grower.

**Week 18 or first egg:** Begin transitioning to layer feed. Don't switch overnight. Mix the feeds over about 7-10 days to avoid digestive upset.

**Ongoing:** Layer feed with 15-17% protein and 3.5-4.5% calcium. Add free-choice oyster shell alongside the feeder regardless. Some hens need more calcium than the feed provides, especially heavy producers.

This schedule applies to standard breeds. If you're raising meat-heavy dual-purpose breeds or high-production layers, the protein requirements can differ slightly. [Dual purpose chicken breeds](/dual-purpose-chicken-breeds/) sometimes benefit from staying on a higher-protein grower feed a bit longer given their heavier frame development.

## Choosing the Right Feed Product

Not all feeds are created equal. Here are a few things to actually look at on the label before buying:

**Protein source:** Soy-based feeds are the most common and least expensive. If you prefer soy-free options (some keepers have concerns about phytoestrogens), expect to pay more. Organic and non-GMO feeds exist in both categories.

**Feed form:** Crumbles tend to work better for younger birds, while pellets reduce waste for adults. Mash is the least processed but also the messiest. A lot of wasted feed ends up meaning wasted money.

**Calcium-to-phosphorus ratio:** You want roughly a 2:1 ratio in layer feed. Too much phosphorus relative to calcium can interfere with absorption.

**Additives:** Some feeds include probiotics, prebiotics, or marigold extract for yolk color. These aren't gimmicks, marigold meal genuinely does deepen yolk color, and probiotics can support gut health especially after stress or antibiotic treatment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I feed layer pellets to chicks if it's all I have?

Avoid it if at all possible. The high calcium content in layer feed is hard on young kidneys. If you're in a genuine emergency situation and it's your only option for a day or two, dilute it heavily with plain grains and get proper starter feed as soon as you can. This is not a long-term workaround.

### When exactly should I switch from starter to layer feed?

The most reliable trigger is seeing your first egg, not a calendar date. Breed matters here since some bantams start laying at 16 weeks while larger breeds might not lay until 24 weeks. Watch for signs of laying readiness: a reddening, enlarging comb, squatting behavior when you approach, and interest in the nest boxes.

### Can roosters eat layer feed?

They can tolerate it in mixed flock situations, but it's not ideal over the long term. Roosters don't need the elevated calcium, and high calcium can stress their kidneys over time. If you have a rooster with laying hens, the practical solution is again: offer an all-flock feed and keep oyster shell available separately. The rooster won't seek out the oyster shell, but your hens will.

### Why are my hens laying thin-shelled eggs even on layer feed?

A few possibilities. Check that the calcium percentage on your feed bag is 3.5% or higher. Older hens, particularly those over 2-3 years, absorb calcium less efficiently and often need supplemental oyster shell even on a good layer ration. High-production breeds like [best egg laying chickens](/best-egg-laying-chickens/) put enormous demands on their systems and almost always benefit from separate free-choice calcium. Heat stress can also temporarily reduce shell quality regardless of diet.

### Is organic or non-GMO starter or layer feed worth the extra cost?

Nutritionally, the evidence that organic feed produces significantly healthier or more productive chickens is limited. What you're paying for is primarily peace of mind around inputs. That said, some small producers who sell eggs at a premium use organic feed as part of their marketing. For most backyard keepers, a quality conventional feed from a reputable brand will give you excellent results without the 30-50% price premium.

Getting the feed right isn't complicated once you understand what each formula is actually doing. Starter feed is about building a bird. Layer feed is about sustaining production without depleting the bird. Keep those two jobs in mind, time your transitions around real biological markers instead of arbitrary dates, and your flock will reward you with consistent eggs and long healthy lives.

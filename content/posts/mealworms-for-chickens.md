My chickens would do anything for mealworms. Truly, anything. I once accidentally dropped a bag near the coop door and had six hens in a full sprint before the bag even hit the ground. That part's fun. What's less fun is realizing, a few months into keeping chickens, that you've been treating mealworms like a staple feed instead of what they actually are.

You're probably wondering whether mealworms are actually good for chickens, how many is too many, dried versus live, and whether growing your own is worth the effort. These are exactly the right questions. But first, let's reset on how to think about this ingredient in general.

Mealworms are a treat. A good one, genuinely nutritious, but still a treat. The protein content in dried mealworms runs around 53%, which sounds incredible until you realize chickens are getting what they need from a [properly balanced layer feed](/layer-feed-vs-starter-feed/). Dumping high-protein treats on top can actually throw off that balance over time. Too much protein pushes extra nitrogen through the kidneys. It won't kill a healthy hen overnight, but it's a real thing to manage.

## Dried vs. Live: There's Actually a Right Answer

| Aspect | Dried Mealworms | Live Mealworms |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cost | $15-$25/lb | $5-$8 per 250 count |
| Convenience | High (no escapes, minimal smell) | Lower (requires containment, live handling) |
| Enrichment Value | Standard pecking | High (stimulates foraging behavior) |
| Best Use Case | Regular treats, year-round | Occasional enrichment, winter supplementation |
| Storage | Shelf-stable | Requires live storage setup |

Most backyard keepers start with dried mealworms because they're easy. A bag of Scratch and Peck Cluckin' Good Grubs or the Hatorian dried mealworms on Amazon runs about $15 to $25 for a pound, depending on where you buy. You can toss a handful into the run without dealing with escapes, smell, or a live colony, and the hens love them.

Here's what I tell people: dried is more convenient, but live mealworms offer something dried ones don't. Enrichment. Chickens are foragers by instinct. Watching a hen hunt down a moving mealworm isn't the same experience as pecking at a pile of dried ones. The live version stimulates more natural behavior, which matters if your birds are in a smaller run and don't have great access to bugs on their own. I use dried probably 90% of the time because I have a life, but I'll throw in a cup of live worms from a local reptile supply store ($5 to $8 for 250 count) once every couple weeks in winter when foraging options disappear.

One thing worth knowing: dried mealworms sold for chickens in the UK and EU are sometimes regulated differently than those sold in the US. In the US, as of 2023, dried insect larvae are generally fine to feed. Just don't source them from somewhere sketchy or use ones sold for fishing bait, which may have additives.

## How Many Is Actually Too Many

This is where almost everyone overfeeds, myself included for the first two years.

The rule I follow now: treats (mealworms included) should make up no more than 10% of your flock's total daily intake. For a standard laying hen eating roughly a quarter pound of feed per day, that's about 0.4 ounces of treats total. A small handful of dried mealworms can weigh half an ounce easily. One enthusiastic handful already pushes you over.

I'm not saying weigh every mealworm. I'm saying understand the scale. A few pinches per bird, a few times a week, is reasonable. Every day, unlimited, is how you end up with fat hens laying soft-shelled eggs.

Overfeeding protein can also contribute to feather picking in some flocks, though the research on the exact mechanism is still a little murky. What I can tell you from watching my own birds is that the year I was most generous with mealworms was the same year I had the most pecking issues. Correlation, not necessarily causation, but it made me pull back.

## Growing Your Own: Honest Assessment

A lot of people get excited about raising a mealworm colony after their first sticker shock at the store. The math looks appealing: buy 1,000 live mealworms for around $20, set up a few bins, and supposedly generate a self-sustaining colony that feeds your flock for free forever.

The reality is messier. It takes 3 to 4 months from larvae to harvest. You need to keep the colony between 70 and 80 degrees Fahrenheit consistently, which in winter means a heated space or a heat mat (the Zoo Med ReptiTherm mats work fine and run about $20). You'll need bins, bran or oatmeal as substrate, and moisture from vegetable scraps. The smell is mild but real. Beetles escape if you're not careful about bin lids.

I ran a colony for about 18 months. Honestly? It was worth it for the experience and for having a live source available without a trip to the store. Whether it was financially worth it depends on how you value your time and attention. If you have the setup already (a heated garage, a spare shelf, a little patience) and you're keeping more than six birds, try it. The [Hatorian 1000 Count Live Mealworms](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=live+mealworms+1000+count) are a decent starting colony. If you're keeping three hens in a small backyard and buying dried worms already covers your needs, skip the colony. Save yourself the drama of escaped beetles in the garage.

Mealworms have a terrible calcium-to-phosphorus ratio. We're talking roughly 1:18, phosphorus dominant, which is basically the opposite of what a laying hen needs. This doesn't mean you can't feed them, but it does mean that if you're already skimping on oyster shell access, mealworms aren't helping.

Keep a separate oyster shell feeder available free-choice at all times. I use a basic wall-mounted feeder from my local farm store for about $8. Hens will self-regulate their calcium intake pretty well when given the option, but they can't compensate if it's not available.

After ten years, mealworms are still one of my favorite tools for bonding with new birds and getting reluctant hens to eat something medicated they'd otherwise refuse. The trick is keeping them exactly where they belong: a small, deliberate part of the routine, not the main event.

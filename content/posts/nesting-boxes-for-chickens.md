A hen that refuses to use her nesting box isn't being difficult. She's telling you something is wrong. Whether it's the wrong size, too much light, not enough privacy, or simply the wrong location, chickens are remarkably specific about where they'll lay. Get the nesting boxes right, and you'll collect clean eggs from predictable spots every single day. Get them wrong, and you'll spend your mornings hunting eggs in corners, under feeders, and in places you'd rather not think about.

## How Many Nesting Boxes Do You Actually Need?

The old rule of thumb is one nesting box per four to five hens. That's a reasonable starting point, but the reality is a little more nuanced. Chickens are creatures of habit, and they're also weirdly competitive about favorite boxes. Even if you have eight boxes for eight hens, you'll often find six of them queued up for the same two.

That said, too few boxes creates real problems. Hens will lay on the floor, break eggs trying to crowd in together, or worse, start eating eggs out of frustration. For a flock of 12 hens, I'd recommend at least three boxes, but having four or five gives you a buffer.

If you're just starting out and thinking through your entire setup, the guide on [how many chickens should I get](/how-many-chickens-should-i-get/) can help you plan your flock size before you build or buy anything.

Community nesting boxes, which are long open troughs divided into sections, work well for larger flocks. Individual enclosed boxes tend to suit smaller backyard setups where you want hens to feel more secure.

## The Right Dimensions Matter More Than You Think

Standard nesting box size recommendations sit around 12x12 inches for most laying breeds. That works fine for Leghorns, Easter Eggers, and medium-sized hens. But if you're keeping larger breeds like Jersey Giants, Brahmas, or Orpingtons, you'll want to bump that up to 14x14 or even 16x16. A hen crammed into a box that's too small won't use it, and she definitely won't be comfortable laying in it.

Depth matters too. Boxes should be at least 12 inches deep so eggs don't roll out and hens feel enclosed enough to settle down. A front lip of about 3 to 4 inches keeps bedding and eggs inside without making it hard for hens to step in.

Height placement is something a lot of beginners overlook. Nesting boxes should sit 18 to 24 inches off the coop floor. Low enough that hens can reach them easily, high enough that they stay drier and cleaner. One important detail: your roosts must be higher than your nesting boxes. Chickens always sleep at the highest point available. If your boxes are the tallest thing in the coop, you'll find hens roosting in them overnight and filling them with manure. Not ideal.

## Materials, Bedding, and What Actually Stays Clean

Wood is the most common material for DIY nesting boxes, and it works well when built properly. Untreated pine or plywood is fine. Avoid pressure-treated lumber since the chemicals can be absorbed by eggs over time. The downside of wood is that it's porous and can harbor mites, especially red mites that hide in cracks during the day and feed on hens at night.

Metal and plastic nesting boxes are easier to clean and don't give mites the same hiding spots. Roll-out nesting boxes, where eggs gently roll to a collection tray after being laid, are popular with people who want to reduce breakage and egg eating. They're more expensive upfront but genuinely reduce daily labor.

For bedding inside the boxes, you've got several solid options:

- **Pine shavings:** Absorbent, cheap, easy to swap out. Works well but needs regular replacement.
- **Straw:** Traditional and hens like to arrange it. Can become matted and hold moisture if not changed frequently.
- **Hemp bedding:** More expensive but highly absorbent and slower to mold. Lasts longer between changes.
- **Nesting box pads:** Pre-cut pads made from excelsior (wood wool) that sit flat in the box. Easy to replace, hold their shape, and don't scatter like loose bedding.

## Location and Privacy: The Often-Ignored Details

Hens want to feel hidden when they lay. It's an instinct left over from wild ancestors who needed to protect eggs from predators. A nesting box in a brightly lit, open, high-traffic corner of the coop will get ignored while hens find a dark corner behind the waterer instead.

Mount boxes on the darker, quieter wall of the coop if possible. Adding a curtain, which can be as simple as a strip of burlap or canvas, across the front of the box makes an enormous difference. You'd be surprised how quickly hens take to a box after you add that small bit of cover.

Boxes should also be accessible from inside the coop but ideally have an external egg collection door that opens from outside. Reaching into a crowded coop every morning isn't anyone's favorite task, and an exterior door keeps things cleaner and faster.

Avoid placing boxes near the main door where cold drafts hit in winter, or directly under a window where summer light pours in. And keep them away from the waterer. Splashing water plus nesting material is a recipe for mold and soggy eggs.

## Step-by-Step: Setting Up Nesting Boxes the Right Way

Whether you're building from scratch or repositioning existing boxes, here's a practical sequence that gets it right:

1. **Calculate your numbers.** Count your hens and plan for one box per four hens, rounding up. A flock of 10 needs at least three boxes, preferably four.

2. **Choose your box size.** Measure your largest breed. Standard laying hens: 12x12 inches. Large breeds: 14x14 to 16x16 inches. Depth at 12 inches minimum.

3. **Set the height.** Mount boxes 18 to 24 inches off the ground. Confirm your roost bars are at least 6 inches higher than the top of your nesting boxes.

4. **Pick the location.** Choose the darkest, quietest wall. Away from drafts, away from water sources, away from the main coop door.

5. **Add a front lip.** A 3 to 4 inch lip keeps bedding and eggs contained without blocking entry.

6. **Install bedding.** Add 2 to 3 inches of pine shavings, straw, or a nesting pad. Fake eggs or golf balls placed in each box signal to hens where they should lay.

7. **Add privacy curtains.** Cut strips of burlap, canvas, or even old feed sacks to hang loosely over the entrance. Leave enough gap that you can still see inside.

8. **Watch and adjust.** Give your hens a week. If they're still laying on the floor, observe where they prefer going and adjust box location or height accordingly.

## Troubleshooting: When Hens Won't Use Their Boxes

Floor laying is probably the most common complaint. The usual culprits are boxes that are too bright, too exposed, placed too high (some hens won't bother jumping), or introduced before hens were trained to them. Using ceramic eggs or golf balls in the box is one of the oldest tricks in the book and it genuinely works. Hens see them, investigate, and start associating the spot with laying.

Broody hens taking over boxes is another issue. A hen that goes broody will park herself in a box all day, blocking other hens from using it. If you're not trying to hatch eggs, you'll need to break the broodiness by moving her repeatedly, reducing her nesting access, or using a broody-breaking cage.

Egg eating often starts with broken eggs in crowded boxes. Once a hen discovers eggs are edible, the habit spreads fast. Roll-out boxes are the most reliable mechanical fix. You can also try darkening the boxes further, making sure the front lip is high enough, and collecting eggs more frequently, ideally twice a day.

Dirty eggs usually come down to two things: soiled bedding and hens sleeping in boxes overnight. Change bedding weekly at minimum, more often in wet weather. And if hens are roosting in the boxes, verify that your roost bars are higher and consider temporarily blocking access to the boxes after dusk until they learn to roost elsewhere.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many nesting boxes do I need for 6 hens?

Two boxes is the bare minimum for six hens, but three is a much better choice. It gives each hen options, reduces competition, and means you're not scrambling if one box gets fouled or a broody hen camps out in another.

### Can chickens share nesting boxes?

Yes, and they often choose to. It's common to see two hens squeezed into the same box while the others sit empty. As long as hens aren't stressed, fighting, or breaking eggs in the process, shared use is perfectly normal behavior.

### What should I put inside a nesting box?

Loose bedding like pine shavings or straw works well. Pre-cut nesting pads are a convenient alternative. Aim for 2 to 3 inches of material so eggs land softly. A fake egg placed in each box helps train new layers where to go.

### Should nesting boxes be inside or outside the coop?

The boxes themselves should be inside the coop where hens feel safe and protected. The egg collection door, however, should open from outside the coop. This lets you gather eggs quickly without going through the main door, which is cleaner and more convenient.

### At what age do hens start using nesting boxes?

Most hens begin laying between 18 and 24 weeks of age depending on breed. You can introduce fake eggs or golf balls to the boxes a couple of weeks before that window so pullets start associating the boxes with laying before their first egg arrives.

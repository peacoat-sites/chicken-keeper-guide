Most natural worming guides tell you to throw some pumpkin seeds at your flock and call it a day. That's not wrong exactly, but it's maybe 10% of the picture, and if your birds are carrying a heavy worm load, pumpkin seeds alone aren't going to cut it.

Here's the full version.

## Why Worms Are a Real Problem (and Why They're Often Invisible)

Chickens pick up intestinal parasites constantly. Every scratch in the dirt, every earthworm gulped down, every drink from a puddle is a potential exposure. The most common culprits in backyard flocks are roundworms (*Ascaridia galli*), cecal worms (*Heterakis gallinarum*), capillary worms, and tapeworms. Cecal worms matter especially because they carry *Histomonas meleagridis*, the protozoan that causes blackhead disease in turkeys. If you keep mixed species, that's a serious issue.

The tricky part: a moderate worm burden looks like a dozen other things. Soft shells. Reduced laying. Dull feathers. Birds that just seem a bit off. By the time you're seeing actual worms in droppings or a bird is visibly wasting away, you're well behind the curve. I had a Rhode Island Red two years ago who dropped nearly half her egg production over a few months before a fecal float at my vet's office confirmed a heavy roundworm load. She looked fine. She wasn't.

A fecal float test costs between $25 and $50 at most avian vets or large-animal practices. Do one once a year at minimum, and some states have agricultural extension labs that'll run them cheaper. It's the only way to know what you're actually dealing with before you throw anything at it.

## What "Natural" Actually Does (and Doesn't Do)

Let's be honest about the ceiling here. Natural dewormers work best as ongoing prevention and for birds with low-to-moderate worm loads. If a bird is already symptomatic, pale-combed, losing weight, or you've got a confirmed heavy infestation on a fecal test, you probably need a conventional anthelmintic like fenbendazole (Safeguard Goat Wormer is the most common off-label option for chickens, dosed at 1 mg/lb for five consecutive days). Natural methods aren't a replacement for that. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something.

What they are good for: keeping the baseline worm load low so it never reaches the tipping point. Think of it as maintenance, not medication.

## The Actual Natural Tools Worth Using

**Pumpkin seeds.** The active compound is cucurbitacin, which is thought to paralyze worms and allow them to pass. Research is limited and inconsistent, but the anecdotal evidence from keepers is strong enough that it's worth doing. Raw, whole, organic, non-salted. I give my flock access to a pie pumpkin or two every fall when they're $2 apiece at farm stands. Smash them open and let the birds tear through them. You can also buy raw pepitas year-round, and a good handful scattered a few times a week is plenty.

**Diatomaceous earth (food grade only).** This one's controversial. DE works mechanically, not chemically. The silica particles theoretically damage the exoskeleton of parasites in the digestive tract, but in practice, research on internal use is mixed at best. A 2011 study in *Poultry Science* found DE-fed birds had lower Heterakis worm counts than controls, which is at least something. I use it mixed into feed at roughly 2% by weight during higher-risk periods like spring and late fall. It also does real work as a dust bath additive against external parasites, where the evidence is stronger. Don't breathe it in, and don't use pool-grade DE. [Harris Food Grade Diatomaceous Earth](https://www.amazon.com/Harris-Diatomaceous-Earth-Food-Grade/dp/B003BDTBTO) is affordable and widely available.

**Apple cider vinegar.** ACV in water (roughly 1 tablespoon per gallon) creates a slightly acidic gut environment that may be inhospitable to parasites. It also supports general gut health and immune function. I can't point you to a study that proves it kills worms outright, but it's cheap, the birds tolerate it fine, and the overall health benefits are real enough that I add it a few days a week regardless. Use raw, unfiltered ACV with the mother. Bragg's is the standard recommendation for a reason.

**Garlic.** Broad-spectrum antimicrobial and antiparasitic properties, reasonably well supported in livestock research even if chicken-specific studies are thin. Crush a few cloves into the waterer or mix dried garlic powder into feed. The birds usually don't object once they're used to it. I wouldn't rely on it as a solo strategy, but as part of a rotation, it earns its spot.

**Black walnut hull powder.** The juglone compound in black walnut hulls has documented antiparasitic activity in other animals. Some keepers swear by it. I've used it intermittently and the fecal results have been good, though it's genuinely hard to isolate cause and effect in a flock management context. Use it cautiously and in small amounts as part of a broader approach. You can find it as a [loose powder supplement on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=black+walnut+hull+powder+supplement).

**Fermented feed.** Regular fermented feed supports a healthier gut microbiome, which is your bird's first line of defense against parasites taking hold. I ferment a portion of my flock's feed almost year-round. It takes about three days to get a batch going, requires nothing but feed, water, and a covered bucket, and the birds prefer it noticeably over dry feed. Lower worm loads are a documented side benefit in pastured poultry research.

Natural supplements alone won't accomplish much if your coop and run conditions are a worm incubator. Worm eggs can survive in moist soil for months to years. Overcrowded runs, wet bedding, and lack of rotation are bigger drivers of heavy worm loads than anything a pumpkin seed can fix.

Rotate pasture or run access if you can. Even a two-section divided run with a few weeks of rest between them makes a measurable difference. Manage moisture too: standing water, perpetually wet corners near waterers, and deep-litter problems are where eggs persist and accumulate. Clean waterers every few days in warm weather.

If your run is a barren dirt patch with 12 birds in it, you're going to fight a losing battle regardless of what you add to the feed.

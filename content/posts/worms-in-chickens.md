You find one of your best layers standing off by herself, tail drooping, looking thin despite the fact that she's eating like normal. No respiratory symptoms. No mites you can see. You worm her on a hunch, and two weeks later she's back to laying and acting like herself. That scenario has played out in my flock more times than I'd like to admit, and every time it reminds me of the same uncomfortable truth: internal parasites are probably the most underdiagnosed health problem in backyard chickens, and most keepers don't catch it until the bird is already in rough shape.

I'll be honest, I spent my first few years keeping chickens the way a lot of people do. I cleaned the coop, watched for mites, fed a good layer pellet, and figured I had things covered. Worms felt like a farm problem, not a backyard problem. Then I had a fecal float done on three of my hens out of curiosity, and two of them came back positive for roundworms. They looked completely fine. That was the wake-up call that sent me down a long road of reading, asking vets a lot of questions, and paying much closer attention to what was actually going on inside my birds.

## The Types of Worms You're Actually Dealing With

Not all chicken worms are the same, and the distinction matters because different worms do different kinds of damage and respond differently to treatment.

**Roundworms (Ascaridia galli)** are the most common internal parasite in backyard flocks. They're large, can reach several inches long, and live in the small intestine. Heavy infestations cause weight loss, pale combs, reduced egg production, and sometimes diarrhea. In severe cases, especially in young birds, they can cause an intestinal blockage.

**Cecal worms (Heterakis gallinarum)** live in the ceca, the two blind pouches branching off the large intestine. They rarely cause serious direct harm on their own. The danger is that they carry *Histomonas meleagridis*, the organism that causes blackhead disease. If you also keep turkeys, this matters enormously. Blackhead can devastate a turkey flock, and chickens are the reservoir host.

**Capillary worms (capillary species, often called hairworms)** are thin as a thread and easy to miss. They can live in the crop, esophagus, or intestines depending on the species. Crop capillaria cause birds to lose weight, look unthrifty, and sometimes develop a swollen or impacted crop. These are nasty because the damage they do can be severe before you even know they're there.

**Gapeworms (Syngamus trachea)** live in the trachea and cause a distinctive symptom: a bird that stretches its neck and opens its beak repeatedly, as if trying to dislodge something. People call this "gaping." It's more common in birds that have access to earthworms, slugs, and snails, which serve as intermediate hosts. Young birds are more vulnerable than adults.

**Tapeworms** do exist in chickens but are less commonly a major problem than the above. They require intermediate hosts like beetles, ants, or earthworms to complete their life cycle. In heavy infestations you'll sometimes see segments in the droppings, though they're easy to miss.

What surprised me when I really got into this was how asymptomatic a significant worm burden can be. A bird can be carrying a meaningful load of roundworms and still eat, still lay, still socialize, and still look reasonably healthy to a casual eye. The drop in condition can be so gradual that you don't notice it until you compare photos from six months ago.

## How Chickens Get Infected

The life cycles vary, but the short answer is: mostly from the ground, and mostly from other chickens.

Roundworms and cecal worms spread through a direct fecal-oral route. An infected bird passes eggs in her droppings. Those eggs contaminate the soil, feeder area, or bedding. Another bird pecks around, ingests eggs, and the cycle continues. Roundworm eggs can survive in soil for years under the right conditions. If you've ever had worms in your flock and haven't taken steps to break the cycle, you can assume the ground is still contaminated.

Earthworms are an important transport host for several species, including cecal worms and gapeworms. Earthworms ingest the parasite eggs from the soil, and when your chickens eat the earthworms, they pick up the larvae. This is one of those genuinely uncomfortable trade-offs in chicken keeping. Foraging on pasture is good for your birds in a lot of ways, but it also exposes them to parasites at a rate that confined birds don't experience.

Birds in constantly rotating fresh pasture have a natural advantage because they're not continually re-infecting themselves in the same contaminated ground. Birds on a fixed run, especially one with bare dirt, are in a different situation. The worm pressure in that kind of environment can be relentless.

New birds are another major vector. I don't care how healthy a bird looks when you buy her. Quarantine new additions for a minimum of 30 days, and consider getting a fecal float done during that window. It costs around $25 to $40 at most vets and it tells you exactly what you're dealing with.

## Diagnosing Worms: What Actually Works

Here's where I'll push back on the "treat everything routinely" approach that some keepers take. Routine worming without knowing what you're dealing with is better than ignoring the problem, but it's not ideal. Different wormers target different parasites, and you're better off knowing what you have.

**Fecal flotation** is the gold standard for a backyard keeper. You collect fresh droppings from several birds, mix them in a flotation solution (zinc sulfate is common), and a technician examines the sample under a microscope. It will tell you whether eggs are present and what type. Many poultry vets, university extension offices, and even some online mail-in services offer this. [Merck Veterinary Manual](https://www.merckvetmanual.com) has a good overview of how fecal flotation works if you want to understand the process.

Watching your birds closely gives you useful clues even without a lab test:

- Gradual weight loss despite good appetite
- Pale or shrunken comb
- Dropping egg production that isn't explained by season or stress
- Loose or abnormal droppings
- A bird that "gapes" repeatedly (gapeworms specifically)
- Finding visible worms in droppings (usually roundworms, which look like spaghetti)

None of these symptoms are definitive on their own. A bird losing weight could have Marek's disease, internal laying, or a dozen other issues. But if you see several of these together, especially in a bird on bare dirt ground that hasn't been wormed, worms should be high on your list.

## Treatment Options: The Practical Reality

The research here is a little frustrating for backyard keepers in the United States, and I'll be direct about why. There are no FDA-approved anthelmintics (wormers) specifically licensed for laying hens in the US. That means any worming treatment you use on a laying hen is technically an extra-label use, which legally requires a veterinarian's prescription and guidance.

The practical reality is that many keepers use over-the-counter products. I'm not recommending you bypass your vet. What I am saying is that you should understand the landscape.

**Fenbendazole (Safeguard)** is probably the most widely used wormer in backyard flocks. Safeguard Goat Wormer at 10% concentration is the formulation most people use. The typical approach is 1 ml per 5 pounds of body weight given orally for 5 consecutive days. It's effective against roundworms, cecal worms, and some capillary worms. It does not treat tapeworms or gapeworms reliably.

**Albendazole** is broader spectrum and more effective against capillary worms, but it carries teratogenic risks if given to laying hens (it can cause defects in developing embryos). Most vets recommend not using it on laying hens or at least advising an extended egg withdrawal period.

**Ivermectin** is sometimes used but has variable efficacy against the worms that matter most in chickens. It's also extra-label and has a withdrawal period you need to respect.

Egg withdrawal periods matter. Fenbendazole has an unofficial withdrawal period most keepers observe at 14 days, meaning eggs laid during treatment and for two weeks after shouldn't be sold or given away. Longer if you're being cautious. Discard or compost them during that window.

A visit to a poultry-knowledgeable vet before you treat is worth the cost. Not every large animal vet understands poultry, so ask specifically if they see chickens before you book.

## Step-by-Step: Dealing With a Worm Problem in Your Flock

If you suspect or confirm a worm issue, here's the approach I'd take, based on what I've learned the hard way.

**Step 1: Confirm the problem.** Collect fresh droppings from at least 3 to 4 birds, ideally from multiple locations in the run. Take or mail the sample for a fecal float. This tells you what you're dealing with and how heavy the burden is.

**Step 2: Talk to a vet.** If the fecal comes back positive, contact a poultry vet. They can prescribe the right wormer for the species present and advise on dosing and egg withdrawal.

**Step 3: Treat the whole flock.** Don't just treat symptomatic birds. If one bird has worms, assume others do too, even if they look fine. Treat everyone at the same time.

**Step 4: Clean the environment.** Remove and replace bedding. Rake and remove droppings from the run. Consider moving your flock to a fresh area of ground if possible. Lime can help reduce parasite pressure in the soil, though it won't eliminate eggs entirely.

**Step 5: Observe the egg withdrawal period.** Mark your calendar. Discard eggs during the treatment period and for the full withdrawal window afterward.

**Step 6: Retest in 3 to 4 weeks.** A follow-up fecal float confirms whether the treatment worked. This step is skipped more often than it should be.

**Step 7: Prevent reinfection.** Rotate pasture if you have the space. Clean waterers and feeders regularly, since worm eggs can concentrate around these areas. Quarantine new birds. Repeat fecal floats 1 to 2 times per year as a baseline monitoring tool.

## Prevention: What Actually Moves the Needle

I'll be honest: you can't fully prevent worms if your birds have access to soil. What you can do is reduce the burden and catch problems early.

Pasture rotation is the single most effective tool, and it's free if you have the space. Giving a pasture area a 3 to 6 month rest dramatically reduces the infective egg population in the soil. Even rotating between two sections helps.

Clean, dry conditions in the coop and run matter more than most people think. Worm eggs thrive in moisture. A muddy run with wet droppings sitting around is a parasite incubator. Keeping the run dry with good drainage and fresh bedding, like a deep litter system, reduces that pressure.

Diatomaceous earth comes up constantly in chicken-keeping circles. The evidence for its effectiveness against internal parasites is not strong. It may have some benefit as a dust bath additive for external parasites, but don't rely on it for worms. The research here is mixed at best.

Fermented feed has shown some interesting preliminary results in reducing parasite loads, possibly because of its effect on gut microbiome and pH. I'd call this promising but not proven at the scale that justifies treating it as a primary control strategy.

The best tool you have is your eyes and a relationship with a poultry vet. Know your birds individually well enough to spot the bird that's not quite right. That early catch is the difference between a mild infection you treat and clear, and a serious infestation that has caused lasting damage.

## FAQ

### How do I know if my chicken has worms without a vet?

You can watch for the classic signs: gradual weight loss while eating normally, pale comb, dropping egg production without an obvious cause, loose droppings, or a bird that gapes and stretches its neck repeatedly. If you find worm segments or spaghetti-like white strands in the droppings, that's a pretty definitive answer. But the most reliable way without a vet is a mail-in fecal float service, which many online livestock health companies now offer. It's cheap, relatively fast, and gives you an actual answer rather than a guess.

### Can humans get worms from chickens?

The worms that infect chickens are generally not the same species that infect humans, and direct transmission is rare. That said, you should always wash your hands after handling birds or cleaning the coop, and don't walk barefoot in a chicken run. Salmonella and Campylobacter are a much more realistic concern than worm transmission, and good hygiene covers you for all of it.

### How often should I worm my chickens?

There's no universal right answer, and I'd push back on anyone who gives you a hard schedule without knowing your setup. Flocks on fixed bare-dirt runs in humid climates may need treatment twice a year. Flocks on well-managed rotational pasture may not need treatment at all in a given year. The honest answer is to monitor with fecal floats and treat when indicated rather than on a calendar.

### Are there natural or herbal wormers that actually work?

Some herbs like pumpkin seeds, black seed oil, and wormwood get mentioned frequently in chicken-keeping communities. The evidence for their effectiveness as standalone treatments in established infections is not convincing. Pumpkin seeds contain cucurbitacin, which has shown some anti-parasitic properties in studies, but not at levels that constitute reliable treatment. I wouldn't use them as a primary strategy if I knew my birds had a real infection. As a supportive addition to a clean management program? Probably harmless.

### What's the egg withdrawal period for fenbendazole?

There's no officially established FDA withdrawal period because fenbendazole isn't approved for laying hens in the US. The commonly observed informal standard among backyard keepers and many avian vets is 14 days after the last treatment dose. Some err on the side of 17 to 21 days to be safe. If you're selling eggs or giving them to people who don't know about the treatment, 14 to 17 days minimum. When in doubt, talk to the vet who prescribed the medication.

Worms are one of those problems that feels invisible until it isn't, and by the time the signs are obvious, you've usually got a bird that's been struggling for weeks. The best thing I ever did for my flock's health was start doing routine fecal floats, not because I always found something, but because the times I did, I caught it early enough to handle it cleanly. Know what's normal for each of your birds, get a fecal float done if something seems off, find a vet who actually knows poultry, and don't let the lack of FDA-approved products make you feel like your options are zero. They're not. You just have to do the work.
